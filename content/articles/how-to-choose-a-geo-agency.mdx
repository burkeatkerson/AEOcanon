**Choose a GEO agency by what it can measure, not by what it can promise.** No
vendor controls what ChatGPT says about you, so the only honest thing an agency
can sell is a disciplined process — crawler access, answer structure, schema,
off-site authority, and measurement — applied consistently against a baseline
they documented before they started. This is the buyer's guide for telling those
firms apart from the ones selling certainty they cannot deliver.

## What does a legitimate GEO engagement actually include?

A legitimate GEO or AEO engagement covers five workstreams, and a firm that
cannot name all five is doing a fraction of the job. The discipline —
whether it is sold as [GEO](/learn/what-is-geo), AEO, or "AI SEO" — has a
predictable shape, because getting cited in an AI answer requires the engine to
reach your page, parse it, trust it, and prefer it. Each of those verbs is a
workstream.

**Technical access** comes first because it gates everything else. That means
confirming AI crawlers can actually fetch your pages —
[a check you can run yourself in an afternoon](/learn/check-ai-crawlers-can-read-site)
— resolving JavaScript rendering problems that hide content from retrieval, and
fixing the robots, redirect, and status-code issues that quietly block bots. If
an agency proposes a content calendar before it has looked at your server logs
or your rendering, it is optimizing a page nobody can read.

**Page-level answer structure** is the writing work: leading each section with a
self-contained answer, shaping headings as questions, keeping extractable
passages tight, and supporting claims with evidence. The
[Princeton-led GEO study (arXiv 2311.09735)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2311.09735)
found citations, quotations, and statistics lifted a source's visibility in
generative answers by up to 40%, while keyword stuffing produced no gain at all.
An agency still selling density over evidence is running
[a playbook the research already tested and rejected](/learn/keyword-stuffing-ai).

**Structured data** and **off-site authority** round out the delivery. The
authority piece matters more than most buyers expect:
[Ahrefs' study of 75,000 brands](https://ahrefs.com/blog/ai-overview-brand-correlation/)
found off-site brand mentions correlated with AI visibility at 0.664 versus
0.218 for backlinks — which is why a serious firm talks about
[earning mentions](/learn/get-brand-mentions-without-links) and
[digital PR](/authority/digital-pr-for-ai) rather than about link volume.

## What are the red flags on a GEO agency sales call?

The red flags all share one root: the agency is selling an outcome it does not
control, or a result it cannot verify. Generative engines are probabilistic,
they re-rank constantly, and no vendor has a lever inside them. Any promise that
implies otherwise is either a misunderstanding of the technology or a deliberate
one.

<Callout variant="warning" title="Disqualifying claims">
  <p>
    <strong>&ldquo;We guarantee you&rsquo;ll rank in ChatGPT.&rdquo;</strong> There is no
    ranking to buy and no placement to guarantee. Answers vary by phrasing,
    session, and model version.
    <br />
    <strong>&ldquo;We&rsquo;ll get you into AI Overviews in 30 days.&rdquo;</strong> Nobody
    controls Google&rsquo;s selection, and authority signals compound over months,
    not weeks.
    <br />
    <strong>&ldquo;Our proprietary algorithm gets you cited.&rdquo;</strong> Ask what it
    does. If the answer is a black box, the deliverable is a black box too.
  </p>
</Callout>

Two subtler flags do more damage because they sound reasonable. The first is
**no baseline audit** — an agency that starts publishing in week one has
destroyed its own ability to prove anything, because there is no before to
compare the after against. The second is **repackaged link buying**: paid
placements described as "AI authority building." Those placements tend to sit on
sites with no editorial standing, which is exactly the kind of corroboration an
answer engine discounts.

The pattern is that credible answers contain nouns and numbers, while red-flag
answers contain adjectives. That is not a stylistic preference — it reflects
whether the firm has an actual method underneath the pitch.

## Should you hire an agency, a consultant, or build in-house?

Match the hire to your binding constraint rather than to your budget. Most teams
discover the constraint is one of three things: they do not know what to do
(expertise), they know but cannot get it done (capacity), or they can do it but
nobody owns it (accountability). Each points to a different answer, and
[hiring before you know which one applies](/learn/when-should-i-hire-for-aeo)
is how budgets get wasted.

        "You want to build internal skill rather than rent it indefinitely",

Be honest about the third column. The fundamentals — answer-first passages,
crawlable pages, accurate schema — are genuinely
[doable without a vendor](/learn/can-i-do-aeo-myself), and they deliver a
disproportionate share of the outcome. We have written a separate piece on
[whether you need an agency at all](/learn/do-i-need-an-agency-for-aeo), and the
short version is: not for the basics. An agency earns its fee on depth, scale,
and the off-site work that is genuinely hard to do alone.

## What questions should you ask on the sales call?

Ask questions that force specificity, then judge the answer by how quickly it
gets concrete. A firm with a real method answers in nouns — prompt counts,
engine names, deliverable titles, review steps. A firm without one answers in
adjectives and pivots to case studies.

## How should a GEO agency measure and report results?

A GEO agency should measure citation share per engine against a documented
baseline, and report at the prompt level rather than the summary level. This is
the single hardest thing to fake, which makes it the most useful thing to
interrogate. Ask to see a redacted report from an existing client before you
sign anything.

The shape above is the point: a good report shows you where you are winning and
losing *by engine and by question type*, because the fixes differ. Losing
comparison queries in AI Overviews is a content-structure problem; losing them
everywhere is usually an authority problem. A blended score collapses both into
one uninformative number, which is why
[per-engine measurement beats blended measurement](/learn/per-engine-vs-blended-measurement)
and why [citation share](/glossary/citation-share) has to be defined before it
is reported.

Expect the agency to explain volatility rather than hide it. Citations move
week to week for reasons nobody outside the model can see. A firm that reports
only the good weeks is training you to distrust the whole report. If you want
to sanity-check their methodology against your own, our guides to
[tracking AI citations](/learn/how-to-track-ai-citations) and
[share of voice in AI search](/learn/share-of-voice-ai-search) describe the same
work in enough detail to hold a vendor to it — and
[the visibility tooling landscape](/tools/best-ai-visibility-tools) shows what
they are likely running under the hood.

## What should the first 90 days look like?

The first 90 days should move from diagnosis to structural fixes to compounding
authority work, in that order, with a measurable artifact at each stage. If a
proposal cannot be described this way, it is a retainer in search of a plan.

The honest framing to insist on is that
[AEO does not pay back on a 30-day clock](/learn/how-long-does-aeo-take).
Structure work can move citations quickly; reputation work cannot. An agency
that sets that expectation up front is one you can trust when the third month
looks flat — and one that will still be doing the slow work in month seven,
which is when it starts to matter. Run your own
[AEO audit](/learn/how-to-do-an-aeo-audit) alongside theirs if you want a second
opinion on the diagnosis, and use
[the Canon](/learn/aeo-canon) as the checklist their scope should map onto.

## What does a fair contract look like?

A fair GEO contract names the deliverables, defines the metric, sets a realistic
minimum term, and leaves you owning everything produced. Term length is not
itself a red flag — reputation work genuinely needs a couple of quarters to
compound — but an open-ended term with vague deliverables is.

<Callout variant="tip" title="Four contract terms worth negotiating">
  <p>
    <strong>Deliverable counts.</strong> Articles, page restructures, and pitches
    per month, stated numerically.
    <br />
    <strong>Metric definition.</strong> Citation share defined in writing, per
    engine, with the prompt set attached as an appendix.
    <br />
    <strong>Ownership.</strong> Content, site changes, schema, prompt set, and
    historical data remain yours on exit.
    <br />
    <strong>Review rights.</strong> Named approval step before anything publishes
    under your brand.
  </p>
</Callout>
