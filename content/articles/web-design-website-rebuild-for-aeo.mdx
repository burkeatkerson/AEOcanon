**A web studio needs a website rebuild for AEO when its own site is a client-rendered single-page app
crawlers can't read, is slow, or lacks per-service answer-first pages and schema — because no content
fixes a foundation engines can't parse.** For a studio, fixing your own site is also the proof you can
fix a client's.

<Callout variant="key" title="Quick answer">
  <p>
    You need a rebuild when your site is <strong>client-rendered, slow, or structureless</strong> — a
    JavaScript-only portfolio crawlers see as an empty shell, no per-service pages, no pricing, missing
    schema. Engines can't cite what they can't <strong>read and parse</strong>, so content layered on a
    broken foundation is wasted. <strong>Fix your own access layer first</strong> — and prove you can do
    it for clients.
  </p>

## Why is the site the binding constraint?

Because [access is the first gate](/pillars/access), and a gate you fail ends the contest before content
matters. If an AI crawler fetches your page and sees an empty shell — because your portfolio renders
only in the browser — or the page is too slow, you're invisible no matter how good your work or your
reviews are. The irony is sharp for a web pro: many studio sites are exactly the heavy single-page apps
that [give a bot almost nothing](/learn/check-ai-crawlers-can-read-site). That's not a content problem
you can write your way out of; it's a foundation problem — and one your prospects can see.

## How do I tell if my site is hurting me?

Run two quick tests, and look for the structural gaps.

<Steps
  items={[
    { title: "The JavaScript-off test", body: "Load a key page with JavaScript disabled. If the content vanishes, AI crawlers likely see the same empty page — a fatal access problem common to designer portfolios." },
    { title: "The speed test", body: "Check your load time. Slow, asset-heavy pages get crawled less and trusted less; speed is part of whether you're readable at all." },
    { title: "The structure test", body: "Do you have a dedicated page per service with pricing, or one thin home page and a gallery? No per-service pages means nothing focused to cite." },
    { title: "The schema test", body: "Is there accurate ProfessionalService/LocalBusiness structured data, or none? Missing or wrong schema leaves the engine guessing." },
  ]}
/>

If a page is empty without scripts, slow, thin, has no [dedicated service
pages](/learn/web-design-service-pages-for-ai), or lacks [clean schema](/learn/web-design-schema-markup),
the site is working against you. A fast, server-rendered foundation with real content is what makes
everything else possible — and what you should be selling.

## Can't I just add content instead?

Only if the foundation is already sound. Adding [answer-first
pages](/learn/answer-first-sentence) to a fast, crawlable site works beautifully — that's the whole
program. But adding content to a heavy, client-rendered, or thin site is building on sand: the engine
still can't read or trust it, so the new pages never get cited. The honest sequence is foundation first,
content second. Get the access layer right —
[server-rendered](/learn/server-side-vs-client-side-rendering), fast, structured — and the content you
publish on top finally has a chance to be found. As a developer you already know this; the trap is
applying it to clients while neglecting your own site.
