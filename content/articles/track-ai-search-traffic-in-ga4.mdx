**GA4 will not separate AI search traffic for you — you build it yourself, by
matching the referrer hostnames the engines send: chatgpt.com, perplexity.ai,
gemini.google.com, copilot.microsoft.com, and claude.ai.** Everything else in
this guide is detail on that one idea: where to put the filter, how to make it
permanent, and why the number it produces will always be smaller than your real
AI visibility.

## Which referrer hostnames do the AI engines send?

When someone clicks a source link inside an AI answer, the visit lands in
analytics as a referral from the engine's own domain — chatgpt.com,
perplexity.ai, gemini.google.com, copilot.microsoft.com, or claude.ai. There is
no special AI parameter and no dedicated GA4 dimension; it is an ordinary
referral, which is exactly why it hides in plain sight among the rest of your
referral traffic.

Two notes before you build anything. First, Google's AI Overviews sit inside
Google Search itself, so clicks from them do not arrive as a distinct AI
referrer — they blend into your ordinary Google organic traffic, which is one
reason AI Overview impact is so hard to isolate. Second, these are the hostnames
worth matching today; treat the list as something you revisit rather than set
once, because engines add and rename surfaces regularly. The behavior of the
visitors themselves — small in number, unusually high in intent — is covered in
[how AI referral traffic behaves](/learn/how-ai-referral-traffic-behaves), and
the concept has its own definition at
[AI referral traffic](/glossary/ai-referral-traffic).

## How do I build a GA4 exploration for AI referrals?

Start with a free-form exploration, because it is the fastest way to confirm the
hostnames are actually appearing in your property before you commit to a
permanent configuration. This takes about five minutes and needs no code, no tag
changes, and no developer.

Here is the regex to paste into the filter. It anchors on the full hostname so
you do not accidentally match unrelated domains that merely contain one of these
strings:

```text
^(chatgpt\.com|perplexity\.ai|gemini\.google\.com|copilot\.microsoft\.com|claude\.ai)$
```

If your property records subdomains or regional variants and you would rather
catch everything at the cost of some precision, loosen the anchors to match any
hostname ending in those domains:

```text
.*\.?(chatgpt\.com|perplexity\.ai|gemini\.google\.com|copilot\.microsoft\.com|claude\.ai)$
```

Run the exploration before you go further. If you see zero sessions from every
hostname, that is worth knowing on its own — it usually means either that you are
not being cited yet, or that you are being cited in answers nobody clicks
through from. Those two situations look identical in GA4 and are told apart only
by [citation tracking](/learn/how-to-track-ai-citations).

## How do I make AI its own channel in GA4?

Promote the same regex into a custom channel group, so AI traffic appears as a
named channel in your standard acquisition reports instead of living only inside
one exploration. GA4's default channel groups scatter these sessions across
Referral and Organic Search, which makes the trend nearly unreadable.

In Admin, under Data display, create a custom channel group. Add a new channel —
call it AI Search — and give it a single condition: **Source matches regex**,
using the same pattern as above. Order matters in channel groups, so place the AI
Search channel above Referral and Organic Search; GA4 assigns each session to the
first channel whose condition it satisfies, and a channel placed below Referral
will never receive anything.

Once the channel exists, everything downstream comes free: it appears in Traffic
acquisition, in comparisons, and as a building block for audiences and
conversion reports. Pair it with a conversion event and you can answer the
question that actually matters to a business — not how much AI traffic you get,
but what it does when it arrives.

One configuration detail catches people out here. Custom channel groups in GA4
apply going forward and to a limited window of historical data, not to your
entire property history, so build the channel now even if you are not ready to
report on it — the sooner it exists, the sooner you have a comparable baseline.
And keep the regex in a document somewhere with a date on it. When a new engine
launches or an existing one changes its surface domain, the only thing standing
between you and a silently broken report is remembering where the pattern lives.

## Why is the number structurally undercounted?

Because analytics can only see the clicks, and most AI value never produces one.
When an engine answers a question using your content, the reader usually gets
what they came for inside the answer — your citation happened, your brand was
named, and no session was ever created. This is not a tracking bug you can fix
with better configuration; it is the shape of the medium.

Three separate losses stack up. The
[Pew Research Center found in 2025](https://www.pewresearch.org/short-reads/2025/07/22/google-users-are-less-likely-to-click-on-links-when-an-ai-summary-appears-in-the-results/)
that users clicked a link just 8% of the time when an AI summary was present,
versus 15% without — so the click itself is rarer than it used to be. Some
surfaces pass no referrer at all, or pass one that gets misattributed, so a share
of the clicks that *do* happen never reach your AI channel. And AI Overview
clicks, as noted above, are indistinguishable from ordinary Google organic. The
underlying dynamic has its own entry:
[zero-click search](/glossary/zero-click-search).

<PullQuote cite="The measurement rule for AI search">
  Analytics counts the visits. It cannot count the times you were the answer.
</PullQuote>

None of this makes GA4 useless — it makes it partial. It is an accurate
measurement of one real thing (AI-referred sessions and what they do) and a
badly incomplete proxy for a different thing (your AI visibility). Judging an AEO
program on the GA4 number alone will tell you it is failing while it is working,
which is the argument made in full in
[can I track AI traffic in Google Analytics](/learn/can-i-track-ai-traffic-in-google-analytics).

## What should I pair it with?

Pair GA4 with citation tracking, because the two measure opposite halves of the
same picture. GA4 measures the visits you get; a prompt set measures how often
engines name you at all — including every answer that produced no click, which is
the majority of them.

The method is simple enough to run in a spreadsheet: fix a list of your
customers' real questions, run it across every engine on a schedule, and log
whether you were named, who else was, and which sources were linked. Turn that
log into a percentage — the share of tracked questions where you appear — and you
have [share of voice in AI search](/learn/share-of-voice-ai-search), the closest
thing this discipline has to a headline metric. The related concept of how much
of a category's answers you occupy is defined at
[share of model](/glossary/share-of-model), and the step-by-step DIY setup is in
[how to measure your AI visibility](/tools/measure-aeo).

A fourth source is worth adding once you care about the technical side: your
server logs. They show which AI crawlers are fetching which pages and how often —
information that exists nowhere in GA4, because crawlers do not execute the
JavaScript that fires your analytics tag.
[Reading server logs for AI crawlers](/learn/read-server-logs-ai-crawlers)
covers what to look for.

## How should I read the numbers once I have them?

Read them as a trend by engine, weighted by quality rather than volume. AI
referral traffic is a small stream that behaves nothing like organic search, and
comparing it to your organic numbers on volume alone will always be
disappointing and always be the wrong comparison.

The reason is qualification.
[Ahrefs found AI-referred visitors converted about 23x better than organic](/learn/does-ai-search-send-traffic)
in its analysis of its own traffic — because by the time someone clicks a
citation, they have read a synthesized answer, seen you named as a credible
source, and chosen to go deeper. That is three steps of qualification a keyword
click skips. So the right frame is revenue per session, not sessions; and the
right response to a small AI channel that converts well is to make its landing
experience better, which is exactly what
[landing pages for AI traffic](/learn/landing-pages-for-ai-traffic) is about.

## Where should I go next?

Go to whichever half of the picture you are missing. If you now have a working AI
channel in GA4 but no idea how often you are actually cited, build the prompt set
in [how to track AI citations](/learn/how-to-track-ai-citations). If you have
citation data but the traffic is not materializing, the question is which engines
your audience uses, answered in
[which AI engines send the most traffic](/learn/which-ai-engines-send-most-traffic).

And if the measurement is telling you that you are barely present anywhere, the
problem is upstream of analytics. Start with
[how to rank in ChatGPT, Perplexity, Gemini, and AI Overviews](/learn/how-to-rank-in-ai-search-engines)
for the engine-by-engine map, or
[how to show up in AI search](/learn/how-to-show-up-in-ai-search) for the
plain-English sequence that gets you cited in the first place. Measurement is
worth building early — but it only ever tells you the truth about the work, and
never does the work for you.
