**AI-generated content can get cited, but only when it's made genuinely original,
accurate, and useful — raw model output tends to be generic, unsourced, and
interchangeable, which is exactly what engines skip.** The deciding factor is the
substance and originality you add, not whether a model helped write it.

## Do engines penalize AI content?

Not for being AI-made — they just reward quality and ignore thin content from anyone.
An engine doesn't ask who wrote a passage; it asks whether the passage is
[distinctive, accurate, and worth citing](/learn/how-ai-detects-low-quality-content).
So unedited AI output struggles not because it's AI, but because it's
[generic](/learn/will-ai-cite-generic-content) — and the same fate meets human-written
filler. The standard is substance, applied evenly.

## Why is raw output rarely cited?

Because it's generic by construction. A model produces fluent, plausible text
synthesized from common sources, so it tends to say what's already everywhere and add
nothing unique — no [original data](/learn/does-original-research-get-cited-more), no
[first-hand experience](/learn/does-first-hand-experience-help-aeo), no real sourcing.
That leaves it competing with infinite substitutes, giving an engine no reason to pick
it. It fails the [Originality pillar](/pillars/originality) on its own.

## How do I use AI and still get cited?

Treat the model as a drafter, not the author. Use AI for scaffolding and speed, then
add what it can't — original data, lived experience, accurate
[sourcing](/learn/does-citing-sources-help-get-cited), and a distinct point of view —
and run it through [real quality control](/learn/quality-control-ai-content). The model
accelerates production; the originality and verification that make content citable
still have to come from you. That's the difference between AI-*assisted* content and
AI *slop*.
