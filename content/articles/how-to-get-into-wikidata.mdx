**Wikidata is a free, structured knowledge base that feeds the Google Knowledge
Graph and helps AI engines recognize your entity — and you get in by meeting its
notability criteria, creating a well-sourced item, and linking it to your
authoritative references.** Its bar is lower than Wikipedia's, which makes it an
accessible, high-value entity signal.

## Why does Wikidata matter for entity AEO?

[Wikidata](https://www.wikidata.org/) matters because it's a structured, machine-readable knowledge base that
search and AI systems draw on to identify entities, and it feeds Google's [Knowledge
Graph](/glossary/knowledge-graph). Where a web page describes you in prose an engine must interpret, a Wikidata
item states your facts as data — your type, founding date, location, key people, and
identifiers — that engines can link to directly. That makes it one of the most
efficient ways to become a recognized [entity](/learn/what-is-entity-aeo),
especially before you qualify for a Wikipedia article.

## How is Wikidata different from Wikipedia?

Wikidata is a database; Wikipedia is an encyclopedia. Wikipedia requires significant
notability and is written as referenced prose; Wikidata stores structured items,
statements, and identifiers with a more permissive — though still real — notability
standard. You may qualify for a Wikidata item well before a Wikipedia article, and
the two complement each other: a Wikipedia article is itself strong [corroboration](/glossary/corroboration),
while Wikidata is the machine-readable backbone. For the Wikipedia side, see the
[Wikipedia strategy playbook](/authority/wikipedia-strategy).

## How do you create a Wikidata item?

Create your item carefully, treating it as contributing accurate public data:

Once your item exists, reference it in your [`sameAs`
markup](/learn/sameas-strategy) so your site and your Wikidata entity point at each
other.

## What gets a Wikidata item reverted?

Wikidata items get reverted for the same reasons bad edits fail anywhere on the
Wikimedia projects: promotional tone, unsourced or unverifiable claims, and
undisclosed conflicts of interest. The fix is simple — state only verifiable facts,
cite references, keep the language neutral, and be transparent that you're connected
to the entity. Accurate, well-sourced data sticks; marketing doesn't.
