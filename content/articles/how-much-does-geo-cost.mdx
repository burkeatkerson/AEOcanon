**GEO is sold five ways — monthly retainer, one-time audit or project fee,
per-article content programs, hourly consulting, and tool subscriptions billed
separately — and the model matters more than the number.** There is no
meaningful "market rate" to quote, because two engagements with identical price
tags can contain wildly different work. What follows is how the pricing actually
works, what moves it, and how to think about payback on a discipline that
compounds slowly.

## What are the five pricing models for GEO?

GEO and [AEO](/learn/what-is-geo) services are packaged in five recognizable
ways, and each one answers a different buying question. Before comparing
numbers, work out which model you are actually buying — a $3,000 retainer and a
$3,000 audit are not comparable purchases, and a per-article program is not a
program at all if nobody is fixing the technical foundation underneath it.

    ["Tool subscription", "Per-engine visibility tracking", "Always — you need measurement in every model", "Prompt × engine pricing scaling faster than expected"],
  ]}
/>

The models stack in practice. A typical serious program is an audit up front, a
retainer or per-article program after it, and a tracking subscription running
underneath both. What you should resist is buying content volume before the
[access and structure work](/learn/how-to-do-an-aeo-audit) is done, because
publishing into a site that AI crawlers cannot render is the most expensive
mistake in the category — you pay full price for content that never enters the
retrieval pool.

## What actually drives the cost up or down?

Five variables move a GEO quote, and only one of them is about the vendor. The
rest are about your site, your market, and your ambition — which is why the same
agency will quote two clients in the same industry very differently, and why a
quote given without a site review is a guess.

The authority line deserves the emphasis it gets.
[Ahrefs' study of 75,000 brands](https://ahrefs.com/blog/ai-overview-brand-correlation/)
found off-site brand mentions correlated with AI visibility at 0.664 against
0.218 for backlinks — meaning the expensive, unpredictable work of
[getting mentioned where it counts](/learn/get-brand-mentions-without-links) is
also the work that matters most. That is uncomfortable for budgeting, because
it is the line item least amenable to a fixed monthly price. A firm that quotes
authority work as a flat, guaranteed volume is quoting something else.

## What do the tools cost on top of services?

Visibility tracking is a separate subscription in every pricing model, and it is
the one cost you pay even if you do all the work yourself. Trackers generally
price on prompts × engines, which is the detail that surprises buyers: tracking
50 questions across five engines is 250 checks, and entry tiers are usually
sized in tens of prompts, not hundreds.

We can only speak to figures we have already published in our own reviews, and
those change often — treat them as a shape, not a quote. As of our reviews,
[Otterly](/tools/otterly-review) starts around $29/month on a Lite tier covering
roughly ten prompts, with a Standard tier around $189/month for a hundred;
[Peec AI](/tools/peec-ai-review) starts around $95/month for 50 prompts across
three models; [Scrunch AI](/tools/scrunch-ai-review) is reported around
$250/month with custom enterprise pricing; and
[Ahrefs' Brand Radar](/tools/ahrefs-brand-radar-review) layers AI indexes on top
of an existing Ahrefs subscription. Enterprise platforms are typically
custom-quoted, which is its own signal about who they are built for.

## How should you think about payback?

Think about payback in terms of citation value rather than click value, because
AI answers are largely [zero-click](/glossary/zero-click-search) by design. The
[Pew Research Center found in 2025](https://www.pewresearch.org/short-reads/2025/07/22/google-users-are-less-likely-to-click-on-links-when-an-ai-summary-appears-in-the-results/)
that users clicked a link just 8% of the time when an AI summary was present
versus 15% without — so a model built purely on sessions will systematically
undercount what you are buying.

The counterweight is quality. Ahrefs found
[AI-assistant traffic converted roughly 23 times better than traditional organic](https://ahrefs.com/blog/ai-search-traffic-conversions-ahrefs/),
which is the mechanism that makes the math work: fewer visits, far higher
intent, plus the unmeasured value of being the name an engine says out loud to
someone who never clicks anything.

Use that as a sizing exercise, not a forecast — the deeper version lives in

## Why does the return arrive so slowly at first?

The return arrives slowly because the two halves of GEO move on different
clocks: structure changes can shift citations within weeks, while authority and
corroboration compound over quarters. Budgeting on the fast half and expecting
the slow half is the most common reason programs get cancelled a month before
they would have worked.

## What should you refuse to pay for?

Refuse to pay for anything the vendor cannot measure or does not control. That
single rule eliminates most of the waste in this market, and it is worth being
blunt about the specific line items it rules out.

<Callout variant="warning" title="Line items that don't deserve your budget">

## How do you decide what to spend?

Decide by identifying the constraint that is actually capping your visibility,
then buying the smallest thing that relieves it. Spending that is not tied to a
diagnosed constraint tends to land on whatever the vendor sells most easily,
which is rarely what your site needs most.
