## What does an AI engine actually do to find you?

It retrieves, ranks, and names. When a driver asks for a tow or roadside help, the engine pulls the
companies it can read, judges which best answer the question and look trustworthy and available, and
[names just one or two](/learn/how-ai-engines-choose-citations). Each step is a filter: if a crawler can't
fetch your page, you're out before ranking; if your page doesn't answer the 24-hour or cost question,
you're out at ranking; if your hours conflict or reviews are thin, a competitor gets named. Being found
means clearing every filter.

## What are the three gates?

The same three an engine — and a stranded driver — apply, in order.

## Why can't customers find my towing business?

Almost always one specific broken gate — and for tow companies it's often the missing site or wrong
hours. If you have no [crawlable site](/learn/check-ai-crawlers-can-read-site), or your hours conflict so
the engine can't confirm you're 24-hour, it can't recommend you for "tow truck near me now." Other common
breaks: thin reviews. The fix is diagnostic — find the *highest* broken gate and repair it first. For most
tow companies, building a real site with accurate hours is the highest-leverage move.

## Does ranking in Google mean AI can find me?

Not on its own. Ranking proves Google can read and rank you — a good sign for the first gate — but AI
citation also needs an [extractable, answer-first page](/learn/answer-first-sentence), accurate hours, and
genuine reviews. A company can rank yet be absent from emergency answers if the answer is buried or hours
are wrong. The overlap is real but not total: clear all three gates and you get [found in
both](/learn/how-aeo-and-seo-work-together).
