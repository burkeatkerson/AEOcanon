**Yes — show an honest last-updated date, because it signals to engines and readers
that the page is maintained and current, which supports freshness and trust on
time-sensitive topics.** The one rule is that it must reflect a genuine revision,
since a bumped date on stale content erodes trust rather than building it.

## Why show the date at all?

Because it's a readable currency signal. A visible last-updated date tells engines and
readers the page is maintained, which supports [freshness](/pillars/freshness) and
trust — most of all on [time-sensitive
topics](/learn/do-ai-engines-prefer-recent-content) where readers want to know the
information is current. It's a small transparency that helps people decide whether to
rely on you, and helps engines gauge currency.

## Publish date, updated date, or both?

Both, ideally. The publish date establishes original authority and the last-updated
date signals current maintenance, so showing both tells the full story. If you can
prominently show only one on a frequently-refreshed page, the last-updated date is
usually the more useful signal. And put accurate `datePublished` and `dateModified` in
your [structured data](/learn/structured-data-for-aeo) so the dates are
machine-readable, with on-page and schema dates kept consistent.

## Can a date ever hurt?

Only if it reveals neglect or lies. An old, never-touched date on a time-sensitive
page advertises staleness, and a freshly [bumped date on unchanged
content](/learn/does-publish-date-affect-ai-citation) is deceptive — both undercut
trust. The fix for either is the same: actually
[maintain the page](/learn/does-updating-old-content-help-aeo), then let an honest date
reflect the real work. The date is only as good as the upkeep behind it.
