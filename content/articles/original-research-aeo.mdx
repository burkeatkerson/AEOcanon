**Original research is the strongest, most defensible citation asset you can build, because it makes you the only place a given number exists.** When an answer engine needs that statistic, it has nowhere else to go. The Princeton GEO study found that adding statistics raised citation likelihood by roughly 29%, and a first-party statistic is one no competitor can match.

The barrier is lower than most teams assume. You do not need a research lab; you need a number worth quoting and the discipline to package it for extraction.

<Callout variant="key" title="Quick answer">
<p>Produce a first-party number through a survey, your internal data, a small experiment, or a fresh aggregation of public data. Then package it as one headline statistic stated in a quotable sentence near the top, backed by transparent methodology, with the figure written in text and a stable canonical URL.</p>
</Callout>

## Why does original research win citations?

**Engines reward statistics, and they reward the source that owns one.** Princeton's GEO research showed statistics lifted citation likelihood by about 29% and quotations by about 41%, while keyword stuffing performed below baseline. A first-party statistic stacks both advantages: it is a statistic, and it is exclusive.

When ten articles repeat the same industry figure, an engine can pick any of them. When you publish a number only you measured, you become the [primary source](/glossary/primary-source) for every answer that needs it. That is durable. Competitors can rewrite your prose, but they cannot republish your data without [corroboration](/glossary/corroboration) pointing back to you. This is the heart of [originality](/pillars/originality) and a major lever on [credibility](/pillars/credibility).

## How do you produce research on any budget?

**Pick the lowest-cost method that yields a number nobody else has.** You are not aiming for an academic paper; you are aiming for one defensible statistic.

## How do you package research for citation?

**The packaging matters as much as the data: one headline stat, stated as a sentence, near the top.** Bury the finding and engines may never reach it; Profound found 44% of ChatGPT citations come from the first third of the page.

Write the finding as a complete, self-contained sentence: "In our survey of 240 marketers, 61% had no AEO strategy as of mid-2026." That sentence is liftable on its own. Place it high, repeat the number in any chart caption, and keep the surrounding [chunk](/glossary/chunking) tight so it reads as a clean [citation](/glossary/citation) candidate. For more on weaving unique numbers into content, see [how to add unique data to content](/learn/how-to-add-unique-data-to-content).

## Why does methodology transparency matter?

**A number without a method is a claim; a number with a method is evidence.** State your sample size, the dates the data covers, how respondents were selected or data was collected, and any limitations. This is what lets an engine, and a skeptical journalist, treat your statistic as trustworthy rather than promotional.

Transparency also protects the asset. When others cite your figure, your stated method travels with it, reinforcing you as the origin. This is the same evidence standard that powers a [citable case study](/citable-case-study), and it is why methodology is non-negotiable for [credibility](/pillars/credibility). Combined with attributable quotes, transparent stats are exactly what [statistics, quotes, and citations](/learn/statistics-quotes-citations-aeo) identifies as citation drivers.

<Callout variant="tip" title="Make it easy to cite">
<p>Give the study a single stable, canonical URL, add a clear "how to cite" line, and offer an embeddable chart or a copyable stat. The easier you make reuse, the more often your number, and your link, travel.</p>
</Callout>
