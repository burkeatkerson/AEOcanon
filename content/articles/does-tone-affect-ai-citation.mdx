**Tone affects citation indirectly — a clear, direct, confident tone makes your
answer easy to lift and trust, while hedging, hype, and rambling make passages harder
to extract and less credible.** Engines don't reward a "voice," but they do favor the
clarity and authority that good tone produces.

## How does tone influence citation?

Through clarity and trust, not style points. An engine has no preference for a brand
voice, but it does pull answers that are unambiguous and credible — and tone is the
lever that makes a passage either. The same plain, scannable clarity [helps human readers, who skim rather than read word-for-word](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/how-users-read-on-the-web/). A direct, confident sentence is easy to
[lift](/learn/answer-first-sentence); a hedged, meandering one forces the engine to
guess at the answer. So tone shapes both [extractability](/pillars/extractability) and
the perceived authority behind the claim.

## What tone works best?

Clear, direct, and confident without overclaiming. State the answer plainly, support
it with [evidence](/learn/statistics-quotes-citations-aeo), and avoid the two
failure modes: wishy-washy hedging that obscures the point, and breathless marketing
language that adds adjectives but no substance. The target is reading like a
knowledgeable, trustworthy source — the kind an engine is comfortable quoting and a
reader is comfortable believing.

## Does formality matter?

Less than clarity does. Conversational writing is perfectly fine and often matches
[how people phrase questions](/learn/how-people-phrase-questions-to-ai); what matters
is that the answer is easy to find, lift, and trust regardless of register. The thing
to cut is salesy hype — swap "the best solution ever" for the specific, verifiable
claim beneath it. Substance gets quoted; adjectives don't.
