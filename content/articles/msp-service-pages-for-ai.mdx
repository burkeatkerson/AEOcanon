**Write MSP service pages AI will cite by giving each service its own page that leads with the answer
to the scope, pricing-model, and who-it's-for questions, in plain language a buyer and an engine can
lift.** One self-contained, crawlable page per service beats a single bloated services page every time.

## Why one page per service?

Because a citation is awarded to the page that best answers one specific question — and a catch-all
services page answers none of them well. When you give managed IT, cybersecurity, cloud, helpdesk, and
backup each [their own page](/learn/should-content-sections-stand-alone), each can go deep on its own
scope, pricing model, and fit — and each becomes citable for its own query. A single page trying to
cover everything is shallow on all of them, so the engine cites a competitor with a dedicated, focused
page.

## What should each page lead with?

The answer the buyer came for, before anything about you.

This is [answer-first writing](/learn/answer-first-sentence) applied to the industry: the quotable
answer up top, the depth below, the proof at the bottom — covering managed IT, cybersecurity, cloud,
helpdesk, and backup, each on its own page answering scope, pricing model, and who it's for.

## What makes a service page extractable?

Plain language and a clean structure. Write the way a buyer asks — "managed IT typically runs $X per
user per month" — not in jargon, and use [question-shaped headings](/learn/question-shaped-headings)
the engine can match to a query. Keep each answer in a self-contained passage so it can be [lifted
without the surrounding page](/learn/nine-properties-citable-passage), reinforce it with
[ProfessionalService schema](/learn/msp-schema-markup), and make sure the page is fast and crawlable.
Answer-first, focused, and proven — that's the page an engine cites and a buyer contacts.
