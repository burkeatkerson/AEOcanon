**An AEO brief defines "done" before drafting — the target question, the answer-first
structure, the evidence to cite, and the original angle only you can supply.** A
good brief is what turns a writer, or an AI draft, into citable content instead of
generic filler.

## What goes in an AEO brief?

An AEO brief specifies everything a writer needs to produce a citable page, with the
two decisive elements — evidence and originality — as required fields:

<Steps
  items={[
    { title: "Target question & intent", body: "The exact question in the customer's words, and whether it's definitional, comparative, decision, or local." },
    { title: "Answer-first structure", body: "Require the complete answer in sentence one, question-shaped headings, and self-contained ~120–180-word passages." },
    { title: "Evidence to cite", body: "Name the specific statistics, quotations, and sources the page must cite, inline — no unsupported claims." },
    { title: "Original angle (required)", body: "The proprietary data, first-hand experience, or defended POV only you can supply. If this is blank, don't draft yet." },
    { title: "Links & freshness", body: "Internal links to include, plus the page's clock-speed and next-review date." },
    { title: "Pre-publish QC", body: "The checklist the page must pass before publishing — so the standard is clear up front." },
  ]}
/>

This is [alignment](/pillars/alignment) (the right question),
[extractability](/pillars/extractability) (the right structure),
[credibility](/pillars/credibility) (named evidence), and
[originality](/pillars/originality) (the angle) — all decided before a word is
written.

## Why does the brief prevent generic content?

The brief prevents generic content by requiring the two things genericness lacks:
an original angle and named evidence. When "the original angle" is a field a writer
must fill before drafting — and it's tested against "could a competitor publish this
exact page?" — there's no path to fluent-but-interchangeable output. The same is
true for evidence: if the brief names the stats and sources to cite, the draft can't
coast on unsupported assertions. The brief is where you make
[originality](/pillars/originality) and [credibility](/pillars/credibility)
non-optional, which is exactly where generic AI drafts otherwise slip through.

## How does a brief steer an AI draft?

A brief steers an AI draft by [constraining the model](/glossary/prompt-engineering) toward the structure and
specifics you want and away from its default genericness. Hand the model the target
question, the answer-first requirement, and the named evidence, and the draft comes
back closer to citable. But the brief's originality and evidence fields still need a
human — the model can't supply your proprietary insight or verify a single fact. Use
the brief to direct AI, then have a person satisfy the gates it sets; the honest
limits are in [AI-assisted AEO content](/learn/ai-assisted-aeo-content).
