## Why do reviews carry so much weight for web designers?

Because they're the off-site proof an engine uses to judge trust — and an owner is betting their digital
storefront on you. An assistant deciding whom to recommend [synthesizes
sentiment](/learn/do-reviews-affect-ai-recommendations) from your Google and review-platform reviews to
gauge whether you're skilled, reliable, and worth the money. For a profession judged on results and
craft, that corroborated reputation is decisive — it's the [Credibility pillar](/pillars/credibility)
made visible, and the [off-site mentions](/learn/mentions-vs-backlinks) that correlate with AI
visibility more than backlinks do.

## How many reviews do I need?

Fewer than you think, fresher than you have. There's no magic count — consistency and recency outweigh a
raw total. A steady stream of genuine recent reviews signals an active, in-demand studio far better than
a big pile from three years ago. The goal is an ongoing flow: ask happy clients at natural milestones —
launch day, the first month of results, a care-plan renewal — so new reviews keep arriving and the
engine keeps seeing a studio that's [currently trusted](/learn/local-reviews-and-ai-recommendations),
not formerly busy.

## Do the words in a review matter?

Yes — specific reviews do more work than generic praise.

Reviews that name what you delivered — more leads, a faster site, more online sales, a launch that went
smoothly — give engines specific detail tying you to those outcomes. They reinforce the [questions
owners ask](/learn/web-design-questions-customers-ask-ai), so a handful of detailed reviews can
outperform a wall of generic ones. Ask clients what to mention, and the reviews start doing your AEO for
you.
