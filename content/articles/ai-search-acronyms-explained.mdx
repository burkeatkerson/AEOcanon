**AEO, GEO, LLMO, GSO, AISO, AIO, and GAIO are, with one partial exception, seven
names for the same discipline: getting your content surfaced and cited inside
AI-generated answers.** Only two of them are worth adopting. This page is the
decoder — every competing acronym, what it expands to, who actually uses it, and
whether it names a real distinction or just a different marketing department.

## What does each AI search acronym stand for?

Every acronym below names the same objective — being the source an AI answer
draws on — with the exception of SEO, which names the older surface of ranked
links. The table is the fastest way to see how thin the distinctions really are.

Read down the fourth column and the picture is unambiguous. One row describes a
different surface. Two rows are the same discipline named by two different
audiences. The remaining four are relabelings. That is not a taxonomy of
competing methods — it is a naming problem.

One column deserves a caveat. "Who uses it" is an observation about usage, not a
claim about origin. GEO is the only term on the list with a documented, datable
coinage — a paper you can read. SEO's origin is contested folklore from the late
1990s, AEO emerged diffusely across marketing writing rather than from any single
source, and the remaining four have no traceable first use at all. Anyone who
tells you confidently who invented LLMO is guessing.

If what you actually want is the head-to-head comparison of the three that
matter, we keep those as standalone pieces:
[AEO vs GEO vs SEO](/learn/aeo-vs-geo-vs-seo) for the full three-way breakdown and
[AEO vs GEO](/learn/aeo-vs-geo) for the narrower pair. This page deliberately
stops at decoding.

## When did each term appear?

The acronyms arrived in three waves: SEO with the commercial web, GEO and AEO
almost simultaneously when generative answers broke the results page, and the
long tail of synonyms in the scramble that followed.

The timeline explains the mess better than any argument about correctness. A term
does not win because it is the most accurate; it wins because it was available
when a community needed a word. SEO won by being first. GEO won its niche by
having a paper. AEO won its niche by rhyming with the thing everyone already did.

## Which acronyms name a real distinction?

Only two distinctions in this vocabulary are real: **SEO versus the rest**, and a
shade of emphasis between **GEO and AEO**. Everything else is naming.

The GEO/AEO shade of difference is worth stating precisely so you can stop
worrying about it. GEO, as defined in the
[2023 Princeton study](https://arxiv.org/abs/2311.09735), is framed around the
_engine_ and measures how visible a source is within generated text. AEO is
framed around the _answer_ and the user question it serves. That is a difference
in vantage point, not in method — no team has ever needed one playbook for GEO
and a different one for AEO. Full definitions live in
[what is GEO](/learn/what-is-geo) and [what is AEO](/learn/what-is-aeo).

## What about the acronyms that collide with other meanings?

Two of these terms are actively ambiguous, and knowing which ones saves you from
a confusing meeting. **AIO** is the worst offender: it is used both as "AI
optimization" and as shorthand for
[Google AI Overviews](/glossary/google-ai-overviews) — so "our AIO strategy" can
mean either the discipline or one specific surface within it. **GEO** collides
with the far older marketing sense of geographic targeting; a media buyer who
hears "GEO" thinks of location, not generative engines.

A third source of confusion is worth naming: **"AI SEO"** is used two ways that
are almost opposites. Sometimes it means optimizing to be found by AI — this
discipline. Sometimes it means using AI to do your SEO work, which is a
production-workflow question with nothing to do with citations. If someone uses
the phrase, clarify which one they mean before you agree to anything. The two
readings imply completely different budgets, different owners, and different
success metrics, so the ambiguity is expensive in a way the others are not.

There is a fourth, subtler collision inside the AEO acronym itself. "Answer
engine" predates generative AI — it described featured snippets, voice
assistants, and [answer boxes](/glossary/featured-snippet) years before ChatGPT
existed. Someone who learned the phrase in that era may hear "AEO" and think you
mean optimizing for position zero on a
[results page](/glossary/serp), which is a much narrower job than being cited
inside a synthesized answer. A sentence of context clears it up, and it is worth
supplying rather than discovering the mismatch three slides later.

## Does LLMO name anything AEO does not?

LLMO is the one synonym with a defensible reason to exist, because it names the
**mechanism** rather than the surface — but it still does not describe different
work. "Large language model optimization" points at the fact that you are
ultimately optimizing for what a
[large language model](/glossary/large-language-model) does with your text:
retrieve it, weigh it, and decide whether to repeat it. That is a genuinely
useful mental model.

Where LLMO breaks down is scope. Optimizing for a model implies you can influence
its weights, and for practical purposes you cannot — what you influence is what
the model *retrieves*, which is the
[retrieval-augmented generation](/learn/what-is-rag) layer that sits in front of
it. The exception is the slow, indirect route: becoming an entity that credible
sources discuss widely enough to survive into the next training run. That
distinction — retrieval you can move this quarter versus training memory you
cannot — is real and important, but it is a distinction *within* the discipline,
not a reason for a separate acronym.

[Ahrefs' study of 75,000 brands](https://ahrefs.com/blog/ai-overview-brand-correlation/)
found off-site brand mentions correlated with AI visibility at 0.664 versus 0.218
for backlinks — evidence that whatever you call the discipline, the levers are
reputation and retrievability. The same finding supports AEO, GEO, and LLMO
equally, which is rather the point: a finding that validates every name in the
category is a finding about one category.

The useful test for any new acronym, then, is whether it predicts anything. A
real discipline generates claims that could be wrong — the Princeton work
predicted that citations, quotations, and statistics would lift visibility and
that keyword stuffing would not, and then measured both. None of the synonym
acronyms has produced a comparable prediction, let alone a benchmark. They
describe; they do not forecast. That is the difference between a discipline and a
label, and it is why this page can compress five of them into a single row of a
table without losing anything.

## So which term should you actually use?

Use **AEO** with business audiences and **GEO** with technical ones, recognize the
rest, and adopt none of them. The choice is a communication decision, not a
strategic one, because the underlying work does not change with the label.

that is the whole requirement.

## Why has the industry not converged?

The industry has not converged because nothing forces it to. There is no
standards body for marketing vocabulary, the two terms with real adoption were
coined by communities that do not read each other's publications, and vendors
have an active commercial incentive to name the category after their own framing.
Terminology settles when a single authority defines it or a single term becomes
so dominant that alternatives sound dated — neither has happened here.

There is also a quieter reason: the acronym debate is a low-stakes proxy for a
high-stakes shift. Arguing about whether it is AEO or GEO is easier than
rebuilding a content program around
[a surface where the click often does not happen](/glossary/zero-click-search).
The vocabulary churn is a symptom of an industry still absorbing the change, and
it will fade the way "inbound marketing" and "content marketing" eventually
stopped being separate arguments.
