**AI answers describe and recommend your brand with no editorial review, so they
can be outdated, wrong, confuse you with a competitor, surface a stale negative, or
hallucinate facts outright.** Managing reputation in AI answers means monitoring
what engines *say* about you — not just whether they cite you — and then repairing
the sources those answers are built from.

<Callout variant="warning" title="There is no edit button">
  <p>
    No engine lets you correct its answer directly. You influence what AI says by
    changing the <strong>evidence it reads</strong> — your site, your entity, and
    your corroborating third-party sources — then waiting for the next crawl and
    retrain. Reputation work here is <strong>source repair</strong>, not a takedown
    request.
  </p>
</Callout>

## Why does AI get brands wrong?

AI gets brands wrong because it assembles answers from imperfect evidence with no
fact-checker in the loop. Four causes recur. **Stale training data** means the model
learned an old fact — a former product, price, or leadership detail — that it
repeats long after you changed it. **Weak or contradictory sources** leave the engine
guessing between conflicting claims about you. A **thin [entity](/glossary/entity)**
— little clear, structured information about who you are — makes you easy to confuse
with a similarly named competitor. And sometimes the model simply
[hallucinates](/glossary/hallucination): it generates a plausible detail no source
supports. [Why AI models hallucinate](/learn/why-do-ai-models-hallucinate) explains
the mechanism. Layered on top is [citation
volatility](/glossary/citation-volatility): the same prompt can yield different
sources and framings on different days, so a wrong answer may appear intermittently
rather than every time.

## How do you monitor what AI says about you?

Monitor with a fixed prompt set run across engines on a cadence, scoring accuracy
and sentiment — not only citations. Pick the questions a real prospect would ask,
run them on each major engine, and log what comes back.

## What's the response playbook when AI is wrong?

The response playbook is to repair the evidence the answer was built from, in order
of leverage — source of truth first, then corroboration, then entity, then
freshness.

    { title: "Strengthen corroborating sources", body: "Make sure credible third-party sources say the same correct thing. One isolated correct page loses to several stale or conflicting ones." },

[Building your entity](/learn/build-your-entity) and [building trust with AI
engines](/learn/how-to-build-trust-with-ai-engines) are the durable foundation —
they make the correct answer the path of least resistance for the model.

## Why does the entity matter most?

The entity matters most because confusion and hallucination both stem from an engine
not clearly knowing *who you are*. When your [entity](/glossary/entity) is
well-defined — consistent name and description, connected to authoritative
references, corroborated across the web — engines can resolve you unambiguously,
attach the right facts, and resist mixing you with a same-named competitor. A thin
entity does the opposite: it leaves gaps the model fills with guesses. Investing in
entity clarity is the single highest-leverage reputation move, because it reduces
the *cause* of errors rather than chasing each wrong answer after the fact. It also
compounds with everything else — a strong entity makes your source-of-truth
corrections land faster and your corroborating sources count for more.

## What about a damaging, spreading falsehood?

Treat a damaging false claim as a crisis path, not the routine queue. When a
specific, harmful, untrue claim about you starts appearing across engines, work two
tracks at once.

<Callout variant="tip" title="Two tracks in a reputation crisis">
  <p>
    <strong>Repair the evidence</strong> — publish a clear, well-sourced correction
    on your own authoritative page, line up credible third-party corroboration, and
    fix the entity — while you <strong>escalate</strong> through each engine&apos;s
    feedback or reporting channel. The first track is what actually changes future
    answers; the second flags urgency. Document instances with dates and screenshots
    so the pattern is provable.
  </p>
</Callout>
