## Where do I find data if I'm not a researcher?

In your own operations. Most businesses sit on proprietary data they never
publish — product usage, conversion rates, support trends, survey responses, pricing
patterns. Aggregated and anonymized, any of these can yield a finding no competitor
has, which is exactly what makes [original research so
citable](/learn/does-original-research-get-cited-more). You already generate the data;
the work is analyzing and publishing it as the [Originality
pillar](/pillars/originality) rewards.

## How do I present it to get cited?

Lead with the finding. State the key number as a [self-contained,
answer-first statistic](/learn/statistics-quotes-citations-aeo), attribute it to
yourself, and briefly note what it measures and how you got it. Put it in crawlable
HTML, ideally near the top of the page. A clear, sourced, answer-first number is far
more citable than one buried in a chart image or vaguely described — make the data
liftable, not just present.

## Do I need a huge dataset?

No — uniqueness beats scale. A small but genuine finding from a modest survey, a
sample of your transactions, or a focused analysis can still be unique and citable,
because the value comes from *no one else having it*, not from size. Just be honest
about sample size and methodology so the claim stays [trustworthy and
verifiable](/learn/does-citing-sources-help-get-cited). A transparent small finding
beats an inflated one every time.
