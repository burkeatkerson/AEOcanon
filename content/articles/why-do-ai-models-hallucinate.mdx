**AI models hallucinate because they are built to generate the most plausible
text, not verified truth — so when the patterns they learned run thin, they fill
the gap with fluent, confident fabrication.** A model has no built-in sense of
whether a statement is true; it only knows what *sounds* right. Grounding answers
in real sources is the main defense.

## What is an AI hallucination?

An AI hallucination is output that is false, unsupported, or invented, delivered
with the same confident fluency as a correct answer — fabricated statistics, fake
citations, wrong dates, plausible-sounding but nonexistent details. The defining
feature isn't just that it's wrong; it's that the model gives no signal of doubt.
The 2023 survey
["A Survey on Hallucination in Large Language Models" (arXiv 2311.05232)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2311.05232)
maps the phenomenon's types, causes, and mitigations in depth.

## Why does it happen at all?

Hallucination happens because of what an [LLM](/glossary/large-language-model) fundamentally is: a next-token
predictor that optimizes for plausibility, not truth. As covered in
[how LLMs work](/learn/how-llms-work), the model generates the continuation its
training makes most likely. Most of the time, plausible and true coincide. But the
mechanism has no fact-checker attached — so when they diverge, the model has no
way to notice.

<PullQuote cite="The core reason">
  An LLM doesn't know what it doesn't know. Asked something its training can't
  support, it doesn't stop — it produces the most convincing answer it can.
</PullQuote>

The first two trace to [training data](/learn/what-is-training-data) and the
[knowledge cutoff](/learn/what-is-a-knowledge-cutoff); the third is why fabricated
citations are a notorious failure mode.

## How is hallucination reduced?

Hallucination is reduced most effectively by grounding the model in [retrieved](/glossary/retrieval)
sources, so it answers from real text instead of guessing. Pairing the LLM with
[retrieval-augmented generation](/learn/what-is-rag) — the approach introduced in
["Retrieval-Augmented Generation for Knowledge-Intensive NLP Tasks" (arXiv 2005.11401)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2005.11401)
— narrows the gaps where a model would otherwise invent, and lets it cite what it
used. Other mitigations help too: retrieving from authoritative sources, asking
the model to cite, using lower [temperature](/glossary/temperature) for factual work, and human review.

<Callout variant="warning" title="Grounding is a reduction, not a cure">
  <p>
    If retrieval surfaces a wrong source, the model can faithfully repeat the
    error. That's why engines weight source authority and freshness — and why
    being an accurate, well-evidenced source is itself a defense against the
    machine getting it wrong. See <a href="/learn/what-is-grounding">grounding</a>.
  </p>
</Callout>

## What does hallucination mean for AEO?

Hallucination is, counterintuitively, an opportunity for good content. Because
engines are actively trying to reduce it, they favor sources that make answers
*safe to repeat*: specific, evidenced, attributable, current. The Princeton
[GEO study (arXiv 2311.09735)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2311.09735) found exactly
this — adding citations, quotations, and statistics raised a source's visibility
in AI answers, because verifiable claims lower the model's risk.

That's the deep logic of the [credibility pillar](/pillars/credibility):
when you show your work, you become the low-risk choice the engine reaches for to
avoid hallucinating. Being trustworthy isn't just ethical — it's how you get
cited. Start from [what is AEO](/learn/what-is-aeo), and see the fix in action in
[what is RAG](/learn/what-is-rag).
