## What does an AI engine actually do to find you?

It retrieves, ranks, and names. When someone asks for a climbing gym or rec center, the engine pulls
the places it can read, judges which best fit the request and look well-reviewed, and [names just a
few](/learn/how-ai-engines-choose-citations). Each step is a filter: if the crawler can't read your
passes and pricing, you're out before ranking; if your hours are wrong, you're dropped from near-me;
if your reviews are thin, a competitor gets named. Being found means clearing every filter — and for
gyms, the readable schedule and visible day-pass pricing are often what's missing.

## What are the three gates?

The same three an engine — and a visitor — apply, in order.

## Why can't visitors find my climbing gym?

Almost always one specific broken gate — and for gyms it's often the schedule and day-pass pricing. If
they're trapped in a [booking widget or image the crawler can't
read](/learn/check-ai-crawlers-can-read-site), the engine doesn't know what you offer or what it costs,
so it can't recommend you for "day pass climbing near me" or "rec center with a pool nearby." Other
common breaks: inconsistent hours, or thin reviews. The fix is diagnostic — find the *highest* broken
gate and repair it first. For many gyms, getting passes and pricing into plain text is the
highest-leverage move.

## Does ranking in Google mean AI can find me?

Not on its own. Ranking and a map listing help, but AI citation also needs your [passes and pricing in
readable text](/learn/rec-center-service-pages-for-ai) and genuine reviews. A climbing gym can show on
a map yet be absent from "best bouldering near me" answers if the engine can't read the schedule or the
reviews are thin. Clear all three gates and you get [found in
both](/learn/how-aeo-and-seo-work-together).
