clearly attributed, and vouched for across the web.

## What does an engine actually trust?

Sources it's confident won't embarrass it. Because an engine [risks its own
credibility](/learn/does-eeat-matter-for-ai-citation) whenever it cites you, it leans
toward content it can verify, from an identifiable and credible author or
organization, that [other trusted sources agree with](/learn/how-many-mentions-to-get-cited).
Trust is the sum of those — a consistent pattern of reliability — which is the whole
job of the [Credibility pillar](/pillars/credibility).

## How do I build it, on-page and off?

On both fronts at once. On-page, keep content accurate and current, [cite credible
sources](/learn/does-citing-sources-help-get-cited), and attribute it to [real
authors](/learn/do-author-bios-help-aeo). Off-page, earn [corroboration and a
recognized entity](/learn/build-ai-authority-from-scratch) so the web confirms what
your pages claim. Engines combine the two, so a trustworthy page backed by a trusted
entity is far stronger than either alone — claim and proof reinforcing each other.

## Can I lose it?

Yes — and it's easier to lose than to build. Inaccuracy, contradictions with
well-established facts, hidden or [fake authorship](/learn/do-author-bios-help-aeo), and
thin or manipulative content all erode trust, and a source that proves unreliable gets
cited less. So treat trust as something to maintain through consistent accuracy and
transparency, not a box you tick once. Reliability over time is the asset.
