**Your content should answer the real, high-intent questions your audience asks
engines about your topic — the ones close to a decision, where you can answer better
than anyone, and where you're not yet cited.** Start from a prioritized question
backlog, not from a list of topics you'd like to cover.

## Where does the question list come from?

From real demand, not imagination. The questions worth answering are the ones your
audience already asks — surfaced from [support logs, communities, and the
engines](/learn/how-to-find-questions-ai-users-ask) — captured in their natural
phrasing. Beginning from a topic wishlist tends to produce content nobody searches
for; beginning from real questions keeps you anchored to the
[Alignment pillar](/pillars/alignment): relevant to what people actually want to
know.

## How do I choose which to answer first?

Score and sort. Rate each question on intent (how close to a decision), business
value (does answering it move your goals), and winnability (can you answer it better
than whoever's cited now). High-intent, high-value, winnable questions go first; the
rest wait or get folded into broader pages. The [content gaps where rivals are cited
and you're absent](/learn/find-content-gaps-for-aeo) are often the highest-leverage
place to start.

## Should I cover questions competitors already own?

Usually yes — if you can do it better. A question competitors are cited for is proven
demand, and out-answering them with a more original, evidenced,
[answer-first](/learn/answer-first-sentence) treatment can win the citation, since
[citations spread thin](/learn/scale-a-qa-library) and no source dominates. The only
ones to skip are questions where a strong authority is entrenched and you'd genuinely
add nothing new. Everything else is fair game for a better answer.
