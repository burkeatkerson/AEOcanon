**For time-sensitive questions, yes — engines favor recent content as a recency
tiebreaker, but for stable topics accuracy and relevance matter more than freshness.**
Recency is one signal among several, so a current page wins when the topic moves
fast, while an older but accurate page can still be the best answer.

## When does recency matter most?

When the answer itself changes over time. Questions about current events, pricing,
fast-evolving tools, or anything framed as "latest" are recency-sensitive, so engines
lean toward fresh sources for them. Timeless questions — [definitions](/learn/how-to-format-a-definition-for-ai),
stable concepts — barely care about the date, because the correct answer doesn't
change. So recency's weight is set by the query, not applied uniformly, which is the
nuance behind the [Freshness pillar](/pillars/freshness). Google describes the same query-dependent behavior in its guidance on [AI features and your website](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/appearance/ai-features).

## Does fresh automatically beat old?

No — freshness is a tiebreaker, not an override. A recent but shallow or inaccurate
page won't beat an older, authoritative, accurate one on a stable topic, because
[relevance, accuracy, and authority](/learn/how-ai-engines-choose-citations) come
first. Recency helps when those factors are close and the topic is time-sensitive; it
never substitutes for being the best, most credible answer. A new page still has to
earn the citation on the merits.

## How do I use recency well?

Match effort to sensitivity. Keep time-sensitive pages genuinely current and signal it
with [accurate dates](/learn/does-publish-date-affect-ai-citation); for evergreen
pages, just keep them accurate and don't fuss over the date. That way you invest
freshness work where it actually moves citation — on the volatile topics — rather than
spreading it evenly across content that doesn't need it. It's the
[clock-speed](/learn/how-often-should-i-update-content-for-aeo) principle applied to
recency.
