## What's the growth model?

A flywheel, not a faucet. Each well-served account produces reviews and referrals — and stays for
years; reviews and [answer-first content](/learn/commercial-cleaning-service-pages-for-ai) earn
citations and rankings; those bring direct inquiries; and the money saved on purchased leads funds more
content and a sharper [niche](/learn/industry-niche-commercial-cleaning-aeo). The lead-broker model is
the opposite: a faucet you pay to keep running, selling the same lead to three companies, that stops the
moment you stop. AEO builds the [compounding asset](/learn/business-case-for-aeo) instead — recurring
contracts that found you and stay.

## Why is recurring revenue central to growth?

Because a commercial cleaning account *is* monthly recurring revenue, and filling the top of the funnel
only pays off if accounts stay. A contract that lasts years is worth many times a one-time deep clean —
and long-tenured, happy clients are exactly who write the [reviews that mention
results](/learn/commercial-cleaning-reviews-ai-recommendations) and refer fellow managers, feeding the
AI recommendations that bring the next accounts. So growth isn't just AEO at the top; it's AEO plus
retention together, each compounding the other.

## How do I lower my cost per account?

By replacing recurring fees with assets that keep paying. A purchased lead costs every time and is
[resold to competitors](/learn/is-aeo-only-for-big-brands); an earned citation, a long-tenured
contract, or a referral costs mostly upfront and pays for years. As your cited, niche-focused, and
retained base grows, your cost per account falls and your revenue stabilizes. And because AI names only
a few companies, a focused, certified, genuinely reviewed company can win recommendations a generic
directory [can't replicate](/learn/how-to-make-content-ai-cant-ignore).
