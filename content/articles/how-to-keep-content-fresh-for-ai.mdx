## What does keeping content fresh actually involve?

Continuous upkeep, not a single push. The core loop is: monitor which pages are
[decaying](/learn/does-content-decay-affect-ai-citation), [refresh them
substantively](/learn/does-updating-old-content-help-aeo) on a cadence set by [topic
volatility](/learn/how-often-should-i-update-content-for-aeo), update facts and dates
honestly, and confirm the changes get re-crawled. That ongoing accuracy as the world
moves is the whole job of the [Freshness pillar](/pillars/freshness) — a habit, not an
event.

## Is this the same as a refresh system?

The tactics are the same; the system makes them repeatable. Keeping fresh is the
*goal*; a [content refresh system](/learn/content-refresh-system) is how you hit it at
scale — turning monitor-prioritize-update into a process that doesn't depend on anyone
remembering. For a handful of pages you can do it ad hoc; past that, you need the
system, or freshness slips the moment attention does.

## How do I make sure engines see updates?

Make the change re-crawlable. Keep updated pages linked, refresh your [sitemap's
last-modified dates](/learn/does-a-sitemap-help-ai-crawlers), and ensure [AI crawlers
can reach them](/learn/check-ai-crawlers-can-read-site) so retrieval-based engines pick
up the revision quickly — an update they never re-crawl does nothing until they do.
Concentrate this effort on high-value pages covering volatile topics, since those decay
fastest and cost the most when stale; stable evergreen pages need far less.
