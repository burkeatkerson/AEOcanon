**Yes — niche topics are often where AEO works best, because a focused expert can
answer specialized questions better than anyone and faces little competition for those
citations.** The audience may be small, but the questions are specific and high-intent,
and being the cited source for them is very winnable.

## Is a small audience a disadvantage?

Not really — AEO isn't a volume game. It's about being [cited for the questions people
do ask](/learn/how-ai-engines-choose-citations), not about how many ask them, so a
small but specific audience posing detailed questions is a good fit. And lower search
volume usually comes with lower competition, which makes those citations *easier* to
win — the opposite of a disadvantage for anyone with real expertise.

## Why are niches good for AEO?

Because specificity wins citations and niches are full of specific questions. Big
generic sources rarely answer narrow questions well, so a genuine expert can become the
[cited source with little competition](/learn/is-aeo-only-for-big-brands). Original,
[first-hand knowledge](/learn/does-first-hand-experience-help-aeo) of a niche is exactly
what engines reward and competitors can't replicate — the [Originality
pillar](/pillars/originality) at its most natural, where being narrow is a strength.

## How do I do AEO for a niche?

Go deep, not wide. [Answer the specific questions](/learn/long-tail-queries-ai-search)
of your niche thoroughly, bring first-hand expertise and any [original
data](/learn/how-to-add-unique-data-to-content), and build [focused authority in that
one area](/learn/build-ai-authority-from-scratch). Being the definitive source on a
narrow topic is both more achievable and more citable than competing broadly — depth
beats breadth, and a niche rewards depth more than almost anywhere else.
