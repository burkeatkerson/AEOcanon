**To show up in AI search, make sure AI crawlers can actually read your site,
write your pages so the answer comes first, and get your business mentioned on
other sites the engines already trust.** That is the whole job, in order. This
guide is the plain-English version — no jargon that is not explained on the spot,
and a link to the technical detail wherever you want to go deeper.

## What does "showing up in AI search" actually mean?

Showing up in AI search means an AI assistant names your business inside the
answer it writes, rather than listing your website among ten blue links. When
someone asks ChatGPT, Perplexity, Gemini, or Google's AI Overviews a question,
they do not get a page of results — they get a paragraph. Your business either
appears in that paragraph or it does not.

That is a genuinely different goal from traditional search, and it is worth
sitting with for a moment. In old-fashioned search you competed for a position.
In AI search you compete to be the source a machine chooses to quote and credit.
The industry name for that work is
[answer engine optimization](/learn/what-is-aeo), and it is a discipline, not a
trick — but the first three moves in it are things you can do yourself this
week.

<Callout variant="definition" title="Two words you will keep seeing">
  <p>
    An <strong>answer engine</strong> is any tool that writes an answer instead of
    listing links — ChatGPT, Perplexity, Gemini, Copilot, Google AI Overviews. A{" "}
    <strong>citation</strong> is when that answer names or links your business as a
    source. Getting cited is the win, even when nobody clicks through. Longer
    definition: <a href="/glossary/answer-engine">answer engine</a>.
  </p>
</Callout>

The uncomfortable part is that a citation often does not come with a visit. The
Pew Research Center
[found in 2025](https://www.pewresearch.org/short-reads/2025/07/22/google-users-are-less-likely-to-click-on-links-when-an-ai-summary-appears-in-the-results/)
that users clicked a link just 8% of the time when an AI summary was present,
versus 15% without. That is the [zero-click](/glossary/zero-click-search)
reality: being named in the answer is now a form of visibility in its own right,
the same way a recommendation from a friend is valuable whether or not you can
trace it in a spreadsheet.

## How do I find out whether AI already mentions my business?

Ask it. Open each AI tool, type the questions a customer would actually type, and
write down what comes back. This takes an afternoon, costs nothing, and is the
single most useful thing you can do before changing anything on your website.

Be specific about the questions. "Best plumber" is not what people ask; "who is
the best emergency plumber in Fort Worth" is. Write ten to twenty questions in
your customers' words — problems, comparisons, "best X for Y", "how much does X
cost". Then run each one in ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Google (with AI Overviews
turned on), and record three things: were you named, who was named instead, and
which websites did the engine link as sources.

## Can AI actually read my website?

Before anything else, confirm that AI crawlers can reach and read your pages —
because if they cannot, none of your writing will ever be considered. This is the
step most businesses skip, and it is the one that most often explains total
invisibility.

Two things commonly break it. The first is robots.txt, a small text file at the
root of your site that tells automated visitors what they may fetch. Many sites
block AI crawlers there, sometimes deliberately, often because a plugin or a
security vendor did it silently. The second is JavaScript: a lot of modern
websites deliver a nearly empty page and then assemble the real content in the
visitor's browser. Human browsers do this fine. Most AI crawlers do not — they
read the raw HTML your server sends and nothing more.

## How should my pages be written so an answer can be lifted?

Write each page so the answer to its question appears in the very first sentence,
then support it. An AI engine does not read your page the way a customer does —
it looks for a short, self-contained chunk of text that answers the question
cleanly, and it lifts that chunk. Your job is to make that chunk easy to find and
safe to quote.

This is a smaller change than it sounds, and it mostly means deleting throat
clearing. "In today's fast-moving market, many homeowners wonder about the cost
of a new roof" answers nothing. "A new asphalt shingle roof on a typical
single-family home in our service area costs [your real range], and the price is
driven mostly by pitch, material, and how many old layers have to come off" is a
sentence a machine can pick up and repeat with your name attached to it. The
pattern, and the openers worth deleting, are in
[how to write an answer-first sentence](/learn/answer-first-sentence).

Two supporting habits do most of the remaining work. Give every claim something
concrete behind it — a number, a named source, a direct quote — because evidence
is what makes a cautious model willing to repeat you. And add the structured
data that describes your business to machines in a format they do not have to
guess at: which types matter, and which are worth skipping, is covered in
[schema types for AEO](/learn/schema-types-for-aeo). If you want
the full standard for a quotable paragraph, it is laid out as
[the nine properties of a citable passage](/learn/nine-properties-citable-passage).

## Why do other websites decide whether AI trusts mine?

Because an engine has no way to verify your self-description, so it leans on
whether other people describe you the same way. Your own website says you are the
best; every website says that. What distinguishes a business an AI will name is
being described consistently, by other sources, across the wider web.

The evidence here is unusually clear.
[Ahrefs' study of 75,000 brands](https://ahrefs.com/blog/ai-overview-brand-correlation/)
found that off-site brand mentions correlated with AI visibility at 0.664, while
backlinks — the traditional SEO currency — sat at 0.218. Being *talked about*
beat being *linked to* by a wide margin. That reframes the work: you are not
building links, you are building a consistent public description of your
business.

In practice that means the unglamorous things: accurate and identical business
details everywhere you are listed, genuine presence on the review platforms your
industry uses, being quoted or interviewed by publications your customers read,
and being helpfully present in the communities where your topic gets discussed.
Community forums like Reddit carry disproportionate weight with several engines,
and none of this requires a link back to you — it requires other people
describing you accurately, in public, more than once. The whole approach is in
[how to get brand mentions without links](/learn/get-brand-mentions-without-links).

<PullQuote cite="The off-site rule, in one line">
  Your website tells engines what you claim. Everyone else's website tells them
  whether to believe it.
</PullQuote>

## How do I know if any of this is working?

Measure two things separately: how often you are named in answers, and how much
traffic those answers send. They move independently, and judging AEO on traffic
alone will tell you it is failing when it is working.

The first number comes from the prompt set you built at the start — the
percentage of your tracked questions where an engine names you. The second comes
from your analytics, filtering for visits referred by the AI tools themselves.
That second number will always look small, because most of the value never
produces a click. The hands-on setup is in
[how to track AI search traffic and ChatGPT referrals in GA4](/learn/track-ai-search-traffic-in-ga4).

## What order should I actually do this in?

Do it in the order the machine encounters you: access, then structure, then
reputation, then measurement. Skipping ahead wastes effort — beautifully written
answer-first pages behind a blocked crawler are invisible, and off-site
authority pointing at a page that buries its answer wins you nothing.

<Callout variant="tip" title="If you only have one week">
  <p>
    Spend the first day asking the engines your customers' questions and writing
    the results down. Spend the second day confirming crawlers can read your site
    and fixing it if they cannot. Spend the rest of the week rewriting the opening
    paragraph of your five most important pages so each one answers its question in
    the first sentence. That sequence produces more movement than anything else you
    could do in the same time.
  </p>
</Callout>

## Where should I go next?

Go to whichever step you failed. If you are not appearing anywhere, start with
crawler access — [check whether AI can read your site](/learn/check-ai-crawlers-can-read-site).
If you are being crawled but not cited, the problem is usually structure, and
[the nine properties of a citable passage](/learn/nine-properties-citable-passage)
is the standard to write against. If you are cited occasionally but your
competitors are cited constantly, it is a reputation gap, and
[brand mentions](/learn/get-brand-mentions-without-links) is the lever.

When you are ready to go engine by engine — because ChatGPT, Perplexity, Gemini,
and Google's AI surfaces retrieve from different places and reward slightly
different things — the map is
[how to rank in ChatGPT, Perplexity, Gemini, and AI Overviews](/learn/how-to-rank-in-ai-search-engines).
And the framework underneath all of it, if you want the organizing model rather
than the task list, is [The AEO Canon](/learn/aeo-canon).
