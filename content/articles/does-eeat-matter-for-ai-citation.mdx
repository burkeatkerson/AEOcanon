**Yes — the qualities behind E-E-A-T (experience, expertise, authoritativeness,
trustworthiness) are exactly what answer engines look for when deciding whom to cite,
even though they don't use Google's framework by name.** Demonstrable expertise and
trust signals make your content safer to quote.

## Do engines actually use E-E-A-T?

Not as a literal scoring system, but the substance carries over. E-E-A-T is Google's
quality concept for human raters and search; answer engines have their own
[reranking and trust signals](/glossary/reranking). Yet both are trying to surface
credible sources, so the practical advice converges: demonstrate experience,
expertise, and trust, and you satisfy what engines reward — making this a real
[Credibility pillar](/pillars/credibility) concern even without the acronym.

## How do I demonstrate it?

Show the substance and back it up. Make real experience and named expertise visible
through [clear authorship](/learn/author-authority), [cite credible
sources](/learn/statistics-quotes-citations-aeo), keep content accurate and current,
and be transparent about who you and your organization are. Then pair those on-page
signals with [off-site corroboration](/learn/build-ai-authority-from-scratch) so the
claim of expertise is confirmed by how the web describes you — claim plus proof.

## Which part matters most?

Trustworthiness, with authority close behind. Because an engine stakes its own
credibility every time it cites a source, it leans toward ones that are accurate,
transparent, and [corroborated by others](/learn/how-many-mentions-to-get-cited).
Experience and expertise feed that trust, but the engine's core question is blunt: is
quoting you safe? Everything in E-E-A-T is really in service of answering that yes.
