## What's the growth model?

A flywheel, not a faucet. Each great sale earns a review and a repeat customer — wardrobes refresh every
season; [readable products](/learn/boutique-product-pages-for-ai) and genuine reviews earn citations for
"best boutique near me"; those bring foot traffic and online orders; and the fees you save versus a
marketplace fund better inventory and marketing. The marketplace model is the opposite: a middleman that
skims every sale and owns the customer. AEO builds the [compounding asset](/learn/business-case-for-aeo)
instead — loyal shoppers who buy direct, at full margin.

## What's the fastest place to start?

Fix the [readable products and sizing](/learn/boutique-product-pages-for-ai) first — it's the
highest-leverage gap — then make hours and policies accurate so you win [near-me and
style](/learn/near-me-boutique-ai-search) searches, and build a steady flow of [genuine
reviews](/learn/boutique-reviews-ai-recommendations). Those win citations within weeks. Then compound
it: turn every happy shopper into a repeat customer (wardrobes refresh), and add [omnichannel and
online](/learn/online-and-local-boutique-aeo) pages so you win the ship-to-me sale too.

## Why are marketplace sales such a bad deal?

Because they skim the margin and own the customer. Marketplaces like Amazon take a cut of every sale
and the shopper belongs to them, not you — so you compete on price against everyone and build no direct
relationship, no data, no repeat purchase. An earned AI citation sends a full-margin sale that's yours,
and that shopper can become a loyal customer who buys every season and tells a friend. As your cited,
direct, repeat base grows, your margin per sale improves — and because AI names only a few shops, a
real, well-reviewed boutique can win recommendations a faceless marketplace
[can't replicate](/learn/how-to-make-content-ai-cant-ignore).
