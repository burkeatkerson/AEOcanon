## Why doesn't classic A/B testing work?

Because there's no audience to split and the outcome is noisy. An engine sees one
version of your page, not two simultaneously, so you can't randomize users into
variants the way you would for a landing page — and [citations fluctuate run to
run](/learn/why-do-ai-citations-fluctuate), so even a split wouldn't give clean
attribution. AEO testing has to control for the volatility rather than randomize it
away.

## What's the workable method?

Sequential before/after. Establish a baseline by [running your prompt
set](/learn/how-to-track-ai-citations) repeatedly, make one isolated change, wait for
the engines to re-crawl, then measure the same prompts again across several runs.
Changing one thing at a time is what lets you attribute the effect, and judging by a
*sustained* shift in [citation share](/learn/share-of-voice-ai-search) — not a single
answer — is what separates a real result from noise. That disciplined iteration is the
[Adaptability pillar](/pillars/adaptability) in practice.

## How long until I see the effect?

Long enough for re-crawl and for the trend to clear the noise. For retrieval-based
engines that's often a [few weeks](/learn/how-fast-does-ai-pick-up-new-content);
authority-driven changes take [longer to compound](/learn/how-long-to-build-aeo-authority).
Measure across multiple runs after the change so normal citation volatility doesn't
masquerade as a result — and resist judging the change the day after you ship it, when
you're seeing variance, not effect.
