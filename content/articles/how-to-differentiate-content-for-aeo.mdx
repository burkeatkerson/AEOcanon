original data, first-hand experience, a clear point of view, or a more complete answer
than anyone else gives.** Differentiation is the whole game, because engines cite the
distinctive source and skip the interchangeable ones.

## Why is differentiation the whole game?

Because substitutes are infinite and [citations spread
thin](/learn/scale-a-qa-library) — no source dominates a topic. When many pages say
the same thing, an engine has no reason to prefer one, so undifferentiated content
competes with everyone and wins nowhere. Differentiation is what moves you from one of
many to the source worth citing, which is why the [Originality
pillar](/pillars/originality) is so decisive: distinctiveness, not sameness, earns the
citation.

## What are the levers?

The originality set, used deliberately. [Original
research](/learn/does-original-research-get-cited-more), [lived
experience](/learn/does-first-hand-experience-help-aeo), a [genuine point of
view](/learn/how-to-have-a-point-of-view-for-aeo), and superior completeness each make
you harder to replace; combined, they make you the obvious citation. The common thread
is offering something [a competitor's page can't](/learn/how-to-make-content-ai-cant-ignore),
so the engine has a reason to choose *you* specifically rather than anyone covering the
topic.

## How do I find an angle on a crowded topic?

Study the gap. Read the current AI answers for your topic and note what they miss, get
wrong, or under-serve — then provide exactly that, using what you uniquely know. Your
data, your experience, an underserved [sub-question](/learn/find-content-gaps-for-aeo),
or simply a clearer, more complete structure can all be the angle. Differentiation
isn't being different for its own sake; it's deliberately supplying what the existing
answers lack.
