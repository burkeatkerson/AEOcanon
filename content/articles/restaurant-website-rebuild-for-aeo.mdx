depends on.

## Why is the menu the binding constraint?

Because [access is the first gate](/pillars/access), and for a restaurant the menu is where you most
often fail it. The menu is what diners search for — "best gluten-free pizza near me," "good ramen
nearby" — but if it's a [PDF or an image the crawler can't read](/learn/check-ai-crawlers-can-read-site),
the engine doesn't know what you serve, so it can't recommend you. Add a slow, browser-only build or
hours trapped in a third-party widget, and even your basics are invisible. That's not a content problem
you can write around; it's a foundation problem.

## How do I tell if my site is hurting me?

Run two quick tests, and look for the structural gaps.

    { title: "The speed test", body: "Check your load time. Restaurant sites loaded with big images and widgets are often slow, and slow pages get crawled and trusted less." },

If your menu is a PDF, your hours are buried, or the page is slow, the site is working against you. A
fast site with a [readable menu](/learn/restaurant-menu-pages-for-ai) and [clean
schema](/learn/restaurant-schema-markup) is what makes everything else possible.

## Can't I just keep my PDF menu?

No — the PDF menu is usually the core problem, not a detail to leave alone. Because the menu is exactly
what diners search for, an unreadable one makes you invisible for dish-level and dietary queries no
matter what else you publish. Converting it to real HTML text is the single highest-leverage move most
restaurants can make. Get the access layer right —
[server-rendered](/learn/server-side-vs-client-side-rendering), fast, with a readable menu and hours —
and the rest of your restaurant AEO finally has something to build on.
