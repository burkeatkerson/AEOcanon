## What's the growth model?

A flywheel, not a faucet. Each great client experience earns a review and a referral; a [readable
menu](/learn/salon-service-pages-for-ai) and genuine reviews earn citations for 'best salon near me';
those bring new clients; and clients who rebook produce more reviews and referrals — while the
marketplace fees you save fund better stylists and space. The marketplace model is the opposite: a
faucet that takes a cut of every appointment and owns the client. AEO builds the [compounding
asset](/learn/business-case-for-aeo) instead — recurring clients who found you and stay yours.

## Why is rebooking central to salon growth?

Because a salon's value *is* recurring revenue, and filling the top of the funnel only pays off if
clients return. A regular who comes back every six weeks for a cut or color is worth many times a
one-time walk-in — and loyal regulars are exactly who write the [reviews that name stylists and
results](/learn/salon-reviews-ai-recommendations) and refer friends, feeding the AI recommendations that
bring the next clients. So growth isn't just AEO at the top; it's AEO plus rebooking together, each
compounding the other. Win clients with citations, keep them with great work, and the flywheel
accelerates.

## How does this lower my cost per client?

By replacing marketplace fees and recurring ad spend with assets that keep paying. A marketplace takes a
cut of every appointment and a paid ad stops the moment you stop; an earned citation, a loyal regular, a
referral, or a [bridal party booking](/learn/bridal-and-event-hair-aeo) costs mostly upfront and pays
for years. As your cited, referred, and rebooked base grows, your cost per client falls and your revenue
stabilizes — and because AI names only a few places, a real, well-reviewed local salon can win
recommendations a faceless chain or marketplace [can't replicate](/learn/how-to-make-content-ai-cant-ignore).
