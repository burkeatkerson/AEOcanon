## Why is WordPress a good starting point?

Because it clears the first gate automatically. Most WordPress setups return fully
[server-rendered HTML](/learn/server-side-vs-client-side-rendering), so an AI crawler
that doesn't run JavaScript still receives your complete content in one fetch — the
opposite of a client-rendered single-page app that can look [empty to a
bot](/learn/javascript-breaks-ai-citation). That makes WordPress a low-friction
foundation for the [Access pillar](/pillars/access).

## What actually determines your AEO results?

What you do on top of the platform. A crawlable page that buries its answer or says
nothing distinctive still won't get cited. The levers are the same on any CMS:
[answer-first passages](/learn/answer-first-sentence), [inline
evidence](/learn/statistics-quotes-citations-aeo), genuine originality, and
[structured data](/learn/structured-data-for-aeo). WordPress makes those easy to
ship; it doesn't supply them.

## Which WordPress choices help or hurt?

Favor a fast, lightweight theme; keep AI crawlers allowed in
[robots.txt](/learn/allow-ai-crawlers-robots-txt); add schema (usually via a plugin);
and keep a current [XML sitemap](/learn/does-a-sitemap-help-ai-crawlers). The main
pitfall is bloat — heavy page builders can produce slow, markup-stuffed pages or
push content into client-side scripts, which hurts both [page
speed](/learn/page-speed-ai-citations) and extractability. Lean and clean wins.
