evidence on the page and how the web describes you.

## Why doesn't claiming expertise work?

Because engines and readers discount self-declaration. The word "expert" is cheap;
demonstrated knowledge is not. An answer engine infers expertise from what the content
actually shows — [specific detail, original insight, accurate
sourcing](/learn/statistics-quotes-citations-aeo) — and from
[corroboration](/learn/build-ai-authority-from-scratch) elsewhere on the web. So the
task is to prove it, which is the core of the [Credibility
pillar](/pillars/credibility).

## What actually signals expertise?

Evidence a non-expert couldn't produce. Named [authorship with relevant
credentials](/learn/author-authority), first-hand experience, original data or
analysis, primary-source citations, and consistent corroboration across trusted
sources all signal genuine expertise. So does depth and specificity itself — content
detailed enough that only someone who'd done the work could write it, which is why
[generic content](/learn/will-ai-cite-generic-content) reads as anyone-could-write-this
and rarely gets cited.

## Does first-hand experience matter?

Yes, and increasingly. First-hand experience produces the specific, original detail
generic content lacks — the part of expertise hardest to fake or duplicate — so it
both differentiates your content and supports citation. It's also the "experience" leg
of [E-E-A-T's substance](/learn/does-eeat-matter-for-ai-citation): lived knowledge an
engine can't get from a hundred interchangeable sources, which makes you worth
quoting.
