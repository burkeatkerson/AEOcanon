## How is benchmarking different from tracking competitors?

Tracking logs who appears; benchmarking turns that into a scoreboard. [Competitor
citation tracking](/learn/track-competitor-ai-citations) records which rivals show up
for each question, and benchmarking aggregates that same data into each player's
[share of voice](/learn/share-of-voice-ai-search) — a ranking of the field. So
benchmarking is the analysis built on top of the raw tracking: it tells you your
*position*, not just whether a competitor appeared on one prompt.

## How do I measure relative share?

Per engine, across your fixed prompt set. Count how often each player is cited and
express it as a percentage of total citations on those questions, then break it down
[by engine](/learn/per-engine-vs-blended-measurement) since shares differ between them.
Pair citation tracking with referral data — AI assistants increasingly appear as a
[traffic source in GA4's acquisition reports](https://support.google.com/analytics/answer/12923437) —
and track the trend over time so you can see whether you're gaining or losing ground
relative to rivals — the comparative view the [Adaptability
pillar](/pillars/adaptability) uses to judge competitive position, not just absolute
citation.

## What do I do with the benchmarks?

Prioritize where to compete. The highest-leverage gaps are the questions where a
*beatable* competitor is cited and you aren't — proven demand you can plausibly win —
so target those with [better, more original
content](/learn/find-content-gaps-for-aeo) first. Benchmarks turn the vague goal of
"more citations" into a specific, ranked list of where to invest, which is exactly what
makes them worth building.
