**The most common AI search optimization myths don't survive controlled testing.**
Schema markup, llms.txt, and keyword stuffing don't lift AI citations, and AEO
isn't just SEO with a new name. This is the roundup — each verdict backed by
primary evidence, with what to do instead.

<Callout variant="key" title="Verdict">
  <p>
    Most "AEO hacks" are self-declared signals or density tricks that controlled
    studies show don't work. What works is the opposite: be genuinely the most
    useful, credible, reachable answer. <strong>Deserve the citation — don't try
    to manufacture it.</strong>
  </p>
</Callout>

## Myth 1: Schema markup boosts AI citations

**False — there's no measurable lift.**
[Ahrefs tracked 1,885 pages that added JSON-LD schema](https://ahrefs.com/blog/schema-ai-citations/)
against ~4,000 controls and found no uplift in AI citations on any platform — and
a small significant decline on AI Overviews. Engines extract from visible,
rendered text, not the markup in your head. [Schema markup](/glossary/schema-markup)
stays valuable for rich results and entity clarity; it's just not a citation lever. Full debunk:
[does schema markup help AI citations?](/learn/does-schema-help-ai-citations)

## Myth 2: llms.txt gets you cited

**False — no major engine uses it.**
[OtterlyAI's 90-day test](https://otterly.ai/blog/the-llms-txt-experiment/) logged
just 84 of 62,100 AI bot requests touching the file (0.1%), and Google's John
Mueller [compared it to the keywords meta tag](https://www.searchenginejournal.com/google-says-llms-txt-comparable-to-keywords-meta-tag/544804/)
— a self-declared signal engines have ignored for years. The
[llms.txt](/glossary/llms-txt) file is harmless to publish, but it won't move
citations. Full debunk:
[does llms.txt actually work?](/learn/does-llms-txt-work)

## Myth 3: Keyword stuffing helps AI visibility

**False — it actively hurts.** The
[Princeton GEO study (arXiv 2311.09735)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2311.09735) found
keyword stuffing performed below baseline — the worst tactic tested — while adding
quotations and statistics lifted visibility +41% and +29%. Engines work on
meaning, not term frequency, so stuffing muddies your passage and wastes tokens.
Full debunk: [does keyword stuffing help or hurt?](/learn/keyword-stuffing-ai)

## Myth 4: AEO is just SEO with a new name

**False — it extends SEO.** Roughly 70–80% overlaps with strong SEO, but a
distinct layer sits on top: [passage-level retrieval](/glossary/passage-retrieval),
[brand mentions](/glossary/branded-mention) weighed over
backlinks, conversational queries, and citation-based measurement. Treating AEO as
a rebrand is why some teams rank well yet never get cited. Full debunk:
[is AEO just SEO?](/learn/is-aeo-just-seo)

## Why these myths persist — and how to spot the next one

These myths persist because they're intuitive hand-me-downs from keyword-era SEO,
and because plausible tactics spread faster than controlled tests. They share a
tell: most rely on **self-declared signals** (schema, llms.txt) or **density
tricks** (keywords) — the kinds of things engines are specifically built to
discount, because they're owner-controlled and easy to game.

## What actually works

What works is the consistent, evidence-backed set: write
[answer-first, self-contained passages](/pillars/extractability); back
claims with [inline statistics, quotations, and named sources](/pillars/credibility);
earn genuine [off-site authority](/pillars/authority) through brand
mentions; keep content [fresh](/pillars/freshness); and make sure
crawlers can [access](/pillars/access) you. That's the whole of
[The AEO Canon](/learn/aeo-canon) — and the conviction behind it, in
[the Manifesto](/learn/aeo-manifesto), is exactly why the myths fail: you can't
manufacture the citation, you can only deserve it.
