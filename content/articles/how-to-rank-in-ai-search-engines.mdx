**You cannot rank in an AI search engine, because there is no ranked list — there
is a retrieved-and-cited set, and your goal is to be in it.** Once you replace
"rank" with "get retrieved and cited," everything about the work makes more
sense: the differences between engines stop being mysterious and become
questions about which index each one reads from, and which crawler it uses to
build that index.

## Why is "rank" the wrong mental model?

"Rank" is the wrong word because an AI answer has no positions to occupy. A
traditional results page is an ordered list of ten links, and your job was to
climb it. An AI answer is a paragraph of synthesized prose with a handful of
sources attached — and there is no second place. Either your passage was
retrieved and used, or it was not.

That distinction is not pedantic; it changes what you optimize. Ranking rewards
being marginally better than the page above you on a fixed query. Citation
rewards being the clearest, most quotable, most trustworthy source *for a
sub-question the engine decided to ask on its own*. A page can be position four
in Google and still be the passage an AI lifts, and a page can be position one
and be ignored because its answer is buried under three paragraphs of
introduction.

## What actually happens between the question and the answer?

Every engine runs roughly the same four-stage pipeline, and each stage is a place
you can be eliminated. Understanding the pipeline is what turns per-engine advice
from folklore into something you can reason about.

Two consequences follow. First, you must exist in the right index before anything
else is possible — which is why crawler access is the non-negotiable first move.
Second, citation happens at the *passage* level, not the page level: a single
strong, self-contained paragraph can be cited even when the rest of the page is
ordinary. The mechanics of the reranking stage are unpacked in
[how AI engines choose citations](/learn/how-ai-engines-choose-citations).

## Which engine should I prioritize?

Prioritize by where your audience actually is, but know that the traffic is
overwhelmingly concentrated in one place.
[SearchEngineLand's referral session data found ChatGPT drives roughly 87–92% of
all AI referral traffic](https://searchengineland.com/chatgpt-ai-referral-traffic-sessions-data-481630),
leaving every other engine to split what remains. Perplexity sits near 2.8%
despite citing more sources per answer, and
[Trakkr tracked Claude referral sessions growing roughly 64x](https://trakkr.ai/ai-search-traffic)
— from about 133 in November 2024 to about 8,528 by May 2026 — overtaking
Perplexity around March 2026.

But clicks are not the only prize, and for many businesses they are not the main
one. Perplexity's citation density gives you far more chances to be *named* per
answer than its traffic share suggests, and Google's AI surfaces reach an
audience that never opens a chatbot at all. The honest reading of the data:
ChatGPT is where the clicks are, Google's surfaces are where the volume of
questions is, and Perplexity is where being cited is easiest to achieve and
easiest to verify. The full breakdown is in
[which AI engines send the most traffic](/learn/which-ai-engines-send-most-traffic).

## Do I need a separate strategy for each engine?

No — you need one foundation and per-engine measurement. The signals that earn
citations are broadly shared: crawler access, answer-first passages, specific
evidence, and off-site authority. Building seven strategies for seven engines is
how teams burn a year producing less than one well-executed foundation would have
delivered.

<PullQuote cite="The per-engine rule">
  Optimize once. Measure seven times.
</PullQuote>

What is *not* shared is the outcome. The engines overlap far less than their
similar-sounding advice implies: Profound measured roughly **11% overlap** between
what ChatGPT and Perplexity cite, and Google AI Mode shares only about **13.7% of
its URLs** with AI Overviews. Winning one tells you almost nothing about the
others, which is precisely why a single blended "AI visibility" number is
misleading and per-engine tracking is not optional. The nuance — and the small
number of cases where a genuine per-engine adjustment is worth making — is in
[should I optimize for each engine separately](/learn/should-i-optimize-for-each-engine-separately).

## How do I check where I stand in each engine?

Run a fixed set of your customers' real questions across every engine on a
schedule, and log whether you were named, who else was, and which sources were
linked. That repeatable prompt set is the only reliable measurement instrument in
AI search, because there is no rank tracker for a surface that has no ranks.

Two warnings about interpreting the results. First, answers vary between runs
even for identical prompts — one reading is an anecdote, and only repeated
readings over time are a trend. Generation is probabilistic, and retrieval sets
shift as indexes update, so treat a single disappointing result as noise until it
repeats. Second,
citation and traffic are different metrics that move independently: you will be
named far more often than you are clicked. The method is written up in
[how to track AI citations](/learn/how-to-track-ai-citations), and the analytics
half — filtering for AI referrers in GA4 — is in
[how to track AI search traffic and ChatGPT referrals in GA4](/learn/track-ai-search-traffic-in-ga4).

<Callout variant="tip" title="One prompt set, every engine, same day">
  <p>
    Run the identical question list across every engine in a single session rather
    than spreading it over a week. Engines update constantly, so results gathered
    days apart are not comparable to each other — and comparability between engines
    is the entire point of running the set.
  </p>
</Callout>

## Where should I start?

Start with the index you are most likely missing from, then fix structure, then
build reputation. For most sites that order means Bing first — because it feeds
both Copilot and ChatGPT's live search, and because plenty of sites that are well
indexed in Google have never been submitted to Bing at all. Google's surfaces
usually need no new access work but do need deeper sub-question coverage.
Perplexity needs its crawler allowed and genuinely current pages.
