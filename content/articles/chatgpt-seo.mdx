**To get into ChatGPT's answers you have to win three different surfaces: the
model's training memory, its live search retrieval, and the context a user hands
it — and only retrieval reliably produces a linked citation.** "ChatGPT SEO" is a
convenient label for a discipline that does not work like search engine
optimization at all. There is no ranking to climb and no results page to occupy.
There is a generated paragraph, and the question is whether your brand and your
URL are inside it.

## What does "ChatGPT SEO" actually mean?

ChatGPT SEO means making your site the source [ChatGPT](/glossary/chatgpt) uses
when it answers a question in your category — not making your site rank. The
distinction is not pedantic. A search engine returns your page and lets the user
decide; ChatGPT reads a handful of pages, composes one answer, and decides on the
user's behalf which brands are worth naming. You are optimizing for inclusion in
a sentence, not position in a list.

That reframing changes what "winning" looks like. There is no position to report,
so success shows up as **presence**: how often ChatGPT names you for the
questions that matter, whether it links you when it does, and how it
characterizes you. This is the same goal the rest of this site calls
[answer engine optimization](/learn/what-is-aeo) — ChatGPT SEO is just AEO
narrowed to one engine, and that narrowing matters because engines overlap far
less than people assume. Winning ChatGPT does not hand you Perplexity or Google
AI Overviews — their indexes, their retrieval, and their trust signals differ
enough that a citation on one predicts very little about the others.

<PullQuote cite="The ChatGPT SEO premise">
  There is no position to win. There is a paragraph, and you are either in it or
  you are not.
</PullQuote>

## What are the three ways ChatGPT can surface your brand?

ChatGPT can surface your brand from **model memory**, from **live retrieval**, or
from **context the user supplies** — and each one is earned by different work.
Confusing them is the most common reason a ChatGPT strategy stalls, because
tactics aimed at one surface do nothing for the others.

**Model memory** is parametric knowledge: the patterns the model absorbed from
its training data and froze at its knowledge cutoff.
When ChatGPT answers without searching, this is all it has. Memory is what makes it say "the usual options
here are X, Y, and Z" — and it is why well-known brands get named in answers they
never optimized for. You cannot edit memory. You can only become the kind of
entity that broad, credible, widely-repeated coverage puts into the next training
run.

**Live retrieval** is the layer you can actually influence this quarter. With
search on, ChatGPT issues queries, pulls candidate passages, reranks them, and
links the sources of the passages it actually uses — the
[retrieval-augmented generation](/learn/what-is-rag) pattern that underpins every
modern answer engine. Because that retrieval is grounded largely in Bing's index
rather than Google's, a page can rank beautifully in Google and be entirely
invisible to ChatGPT.

**User-supplied context** is the surface almost nobody plans for. When someone
pastes your URL or asks ChatGPT to compare you against a named competitor,
ChatGPT fetches and reads that material directly — a model reading your page in
front of a buyer with intent. The clarity of your own pages is a sales asset, not
just a marketing one.

<Callout variant="insight" title="Why the three surfaces need three strategies">
  <p>
    Memory rewards <strong>being widely known</strong> and pays off over years.
    Retrieval rewards <strong>being crawlable and quotable</strong> and pays off
    in weeks. User context rewards <strong>being legible on your own site</strong>
    and pays off the moment a prospect pastes your link. A program that only does
    on-page work is optimizing one third of the problem.
  </p>
</Callout>

## Which OpenAI crawler actually matters?

**OAI-SearchBot is the crawler that determines whether ChatGPT can cite you.**
OpenAI runs three separate crawlers with three separate jobs, and they share a
company but not a user-agent string — so a robots.txt rule for one says nothing
about the others.

<ComparisonTable
  rowHeaders
  caption="OpenAI's three crawlers and what each one controls"
  columns={["User-agent", "Job", "Blocking it means…", "Affects citations?"]}
  rows={[
    ["GPTBot", "Collects content for model training", "Your content is not used to train future models", "No"],
    ["OAI-SearchBot", "Indexes pages for ChatGPT's search feature", "You cannot be retrieved or cited in ChatGPT answers", "Yes — directly"],
    ["ChatGPT-User", "Fetches a single page on demand when a user references it", "A user who pastes your URL gets nothing back", "Yes — for user-initiated lookups"],
  ]}
/>

The practical consequence is a decision most site owners get backwards.
[GPTBot](/glossary/gptbot) is a **rights** decision: allowing it permits training
use and does not directly earn you anything, while blocking it costs you nothing
in ChatGPT's search results. [OAI-SearchBot](/glossary/oai-searchbot) is a
**visibility** decision, and blocking it removes you from the answers entirely.
Teams that blanket-block "OpenAI" take out their own citation channel by
accident, and the damage is invisible — no error, no alert, just a slow absence
from answers nobody was measuring. Audit your robots.txt specifically for this;
the rule was often added years ago by someone who has since left. The full
decision, including the Anthropic and Google equivalents, is in
[should I block AI crawlers?](/learn/should-i-block-ai-crawlers).

For the complete user-agent list, use the
[AI crawler user agents cheat sheet](/learn/ai-crawler-user-agents-cheat-sheet),
then set your policy with
[which AI crawlers to allow](/learn/which-ai-crawlers-to-allow).

## How do you make sure ChatGPT can reach your site?

You make sure ChatGPT can reach you by confirming three things in order: the
robots.txt rules permit the right bots, the server actually serves them, and the
page's substance exists in the HTML before any JavaScript runs. Access is
binary — nothing downstream matters if the fetch fails.

The order matters, because each step is a precondition for the next and every
failure looks identical from the outside: no citations. Work top-down and you
find the broken gate in an afternoon; guess, and you can spend a quarter
rewriting content no crawler was ever able to fetch. If your logs are empty,
start with
[why isn't GPTBot crawling my site?](/learn/why-isnt-gptbot-crawling-my-site),
which covers the same diagnostic tree for every OpenAI user-agent. This whole
layer is the [access](/pillars/access) pillar — the price of admission, not a
competitive advantage.

## What actually gets a source cited in a ChatGPT answer?

ChatGPT cites the **passage** it used, not your page and not your domain — so the
unit of optimization is a self-contained paragraph that answers one question
completely and carries its own evidence. A single strong passage on an otherwise
ordinary page can earn a linked citation, and an excellent page can be skipped
entirely if its best answer is buried under three paragraphs of preamble.

The qualities that get a passage lifted are the ones that make it safe to repeat.
The [Princeton GEO study (arXiv 2311.09735)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2311.09735)
tested this directly and found that adding authoritative citations, direct
quotations, and relevant statistics lifted a source's visibility in generative
answers by up to 40%, while keyword stuffing produced no gain at all. A model
choosing what to quote is managing risk: an evidenced sentence with a named
source is cheaper to repeat than an unsupported assertion.

<Callout variant="key" title="The citable passage, in one shape">
  <p>
    Question-shaped heading. First sentence answers it completely, in language
    that survives being lifted out of context. Next two or three sentences supply
    a statistic, a named source, or a quotation. Whole block is self-contained —
    it makes sense with nothing above it and nothing below it.
  </p>
</Callout>

Placement compounds the effect. Answers near the top of a page are retrieved and
lifted disproportionately often, which is why the
[extractability](/pillars/extractability) pillar insists you write the sentence
you want quoted and then put it first. The tactical, ChatGPT-specific checklist
lives in
[how do I get cited by ChatGPT?](/learn/how-to-get-cited-by-chatgpt) — the
hands-on companion to this strategic overview.

## How do you get into ChatGPT's model memory?

You get into model memory the slow way: by becoming an entity that credible
sources talk about repeatedly, in enough places that the pattern survives
training. There is no submission form and no lever, which frustrates people used
to the immediacy of technical SEO — but it is also the most durable form of
visibility, because memory keeps naming you even when no search is run.

The strongest available evidence points at off-site reputation rather than links.
[Ahrefs' study of 75,000 brands](https://ahrefs.com/blog/ai-overview-brand-correlation/)
found brand mentions correlated with AI visibility at 0.664 versus 0.218 for
backlinks — a striking inversion of the classic SEO hierarchy. For ChatGPT
specifically, that work does double duty: the same coverage that helps you get
retrieved today is the coverage that becomes memory tomorrow.

<Callout variant="tip" title="Where memory-building effort actually goes">
  <p>
    Into the places your category is genuinely discussed — communities, industry
    publications, comparison and review platforms, and a correct, well-cited
    encyclopedia entity where you are notable. None of it looks like SEO. All of
    it is what makes a model &quot;know&quot; you.
  </p>
</Callout>

The community side is worth singling out, because ChatGPT leans on forum
discussion more than most publishers expect —
[do I need to be on Reddit to get cited by AI?](/learn/reddit-ai-citations) covers
what genuine participation looks like and what gets you ignored. All of this is
the [authority](/pillars/authority) pillar, and it is worth being honest
about the timeline: retrieval fixes land in weeks, memory shifts over model
generations. Budget accordingly rather than expecting the second from the first.

## How do you check whether you appear in ChatGPT?

You check by running a fixed set of real questions on a schedule and logging what
comes back — not by searching your own brand name once and drawing conclusions.
Asking ChatGPT "what do you know about my company" tells you almost nothing,
because it tests recall of a name you supplied rather than whether you are the
answer to a question a buyer would actually ask.

## Should you treat ChatGPT as its own program?

Treat ChatGPT as its own **measurement** surface but not its own **content**
program. The content that earns a ChatGPT citation — answer-first, evidenced,
crawlable, authoritative — is the same content that earns one from Perplexity or
an AI Overview. What is genuinely ChatGPT-specific is the crawler policy, the
Bing dependency, and the fact that you have to score it separately because
citation overlap between engines is low.

good news: access failures are the cheapest thing here to fix and the only ones
that can take you from zero to visible in a single deploy.
