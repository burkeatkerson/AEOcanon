## What does an AI engine actually do to find you?

It retrieves, ranks, and names. When someone needs flowers, the engine pulls the shops it can read,
judges which best fit the request and look well-reviewed, and [names just a
few](/learn/how-ai-engines-choose-citations). Each step is a filter: if the crawler can't read your
arrangements, you're out before ranking; if your same-day cutoff is wrong, you're dropped from urgent
queries; if your reviews are thin, a competitor gets named. Being found means clearing every filter —
and for florists, the readable arrangements and delivery info are often what's missing.

## What are the three gates?

The same three an engine — and a buyer — apply, in order.

## Why can't buyers find my flower shop?

Almost always one specific broken gate — and for florists it's usually the image-only site. If your
arrangements and delivery info live only in [photos the crawler can't read](/learn/check-ai-crawlers-can-read-site),
the engine doesn't know what you offer or where you deliver, so it can't recommend you for "same-day
sympathy flowers near me" or "florist that delivers to [town]." Other common breaks: an inconsistent
same-day cutoff, or thin reviews. The fix is diagnostic — find the *highest* broken gate and repair it
first. For most florists, putting arrangements and delivery info in real text is the highest-leverage
move.

## Does ranking in Google mean AI can find me?

Not on its own. Ranking and a map listing help, but AI citation also needs your [arrangements and
delivery info in readable text](/learn/florist-product-pages-for-ai) and genuine reviews. A shop can
show on a map yet be absent from "best florist for sympathy flowers near me" answers if the engine
can't read the site or the reviews are thin. Clear all three gates and you get [found in
both](/learn/how-aeo-and-seo-work-together).
