**llms.txt is a proposed standard file — written in markdown, served at your
domain root — that hands AI systems a curated map of your site's most important
content.** It is not a permission file and not a crawl manifest. It is an
editorial index: your best pages, listed as links with one-line descriptions, in
the plainest format a language model can read.

This guide covers what the proposal actually specifies, who wrote it, how it
differs from the two files people confuse it with, what `llms-full.txt` adds, and
exactly how to write a well-formed one. For the harder question — whether any of
this moves your visibility — read the companion piece,
[does llms.txt actually work?](/learn/does-llms-txt-work), which owns the verdict.

## What exactly is llms.txt?

llms.txt is a plain markdown file, served at `https://example.com/llms.txt`, that
describes what your site contains and points at the pages worth reading. The
format is deliberately minimal: an H1 with the site or project name, an optional
one-line summary in a blockquote, optional prose context, then H2 sections each
containing a bulleted list of markdown links with short descriptions.

The reasoning behind it is mechanical rather than ideological. Language models
work inside a finite context window, and a modern web page is mostly not content —
it is navigation, scripts, cookie banners, related-post modules, and analytics. A
model that fetches your homepage spends most of its budget on markup it will
discard. A markdown index costs a few hundred tokens and tells it where the actual
answers live. That is the entire pitch.

It is worth being precise about what llms.txt does *not* claim to be. It is not a
ranking signal, not enforceable, not a substitute for having your pages crawled,
and not a mechanism for hiding anything. It is a suggestion, published in the
open, that a system may or may not choose to read.

## Who proposed llms.txt, and why?

llms.txt was proposed in **September 2024 by Jeremy Howard**, co-founder of
Answer.AI and fast.ai, and published as an open specification at
[llmstxt.org](https://llmstxt.org/). Howard's stated problem was that context
windows are too small to hold a whole site and that HTML is a poor delivery format
for machines — so site owners should be able to offer a clean, curated alternative
rather than hoping a crawler infers structure from markup.

The proposal spread fastest through developer documentation, where the pain is
sharpest: an engineer asking a model about a library wants the library's docs, not
its marketing site. Documentation platforms began emitting llms.txt files
automatically, and the convention of serving markdown versions of pages — often by
appending `.md` to a URL — grew alongside it. That origin explains a lot about the
file's shape. It was designed for docs, and it still reads best on sites that are
essentially a documentation tree.

<Callout variant="insight" title="Why the format is markdown, not XML">
  <p>
    Sitemaps are XML because they are consumed by parsers. llms.txt is markdown
    because it is consumed by language models, which were trained on enormous
    volumes of markdown and handle it natively. The format choice is the clearest
    signal of who the file is for.
  </p>
</Callout>

## How is llms.txt different from robots.txt and sitemap.xml?

The three files answer three different questions: robots.txt answers *may you
fetch this*, sitemap.xml answers *what URLs exist*, and llms.txt answers *what is
worth reading*. They are complements, not alternatives, and only one of them is an
established standard that engines actually obey.

The distinction matters because the three are routinely conflated in AEO advice.
[robots.txt](/glossary/robots-txt) is the access layer — it is where you decide
which AI user-agents may fetch your pages at all, which is a genuine visibility
decision covered in
[how to allow AI crawlers in robots.txt](/learn/allow-ai-crawlers-robots-txt) and
[which AI crawlers to allow](/learn/which-ai-crawlers-to-allow). Get that wrong
and nothing else matters, because an engine cannot cite a page it was forbidden to
read. If you would rather not hand-write it, use the
[robots.txt generator for AI crawlers](/tools/robots-txt-generator-ai-crawlers).

Sitemaps sit one layer up: they enumerate URLs and freshness for crawlers that
already have permission. Whether that helps AI specifically is its own question,
answered in
[does a sitemap help AI crawlers?](/learn/does-a-sitemap-help-ai-crawlers) and
[the XML sitemap fields that matter](/learn/xml-sitemap-fields-that-matter).

llms.txt sits one layer above that again — pure editorial curation, with no
enforcement and no established consumer. It is the only one of the three that is
still a proposal.

## What is llms-full.txt?

`llms-full.txt` is the companion convention that inlines your entire
documentation body into a single markdown file, rather than linking out to it.
Where llms.txt is a table of contents, llms-full.txt is the whole book — one
fetch, no link-following, everything a model needs in one context load.

The trade-off is size. An index for a large site might be two hundred lines; the
full text of the same site could be hundreds of thousands of tokens, which exceeds
what many models can hold and costs real money to process when they can. In
practice, llms-full.txt makes sense for tightly scoped documentation — an API
reference, a library, a product manual — and makes very little sense for a
content site with hundreds of long articles.

<Callout variant="tip" title="Which file should you publish?">
  <p>
    Publish <code>llms.txt</code> if your site is a collection of pages worth
    navigating. Add <code>llms-full.txt</code> only if the entire corpus is small
    enough to be useful as one document — a docs site, not a blog archive. There
    is no benefit to publishing a 400,000-token file nothing can ingest.
  </p>
</Callout>

## How do you write an llms.txt file?

You write llms.txt as a markdown document with four parts, in order: an H1 name, a
blockquote summary, optional context prose, and H2-headed lists of links with
descriptions. Here is a well-formed example, modeled on the file this site
publishes at `/llms.txt`.

```markdown
# AEO Canon

> The definitive resource for answer engine optimization — how to be found,
> read, and cited by AI answer engines.

All content is answer-first and structured for extraction. Articles are the
single source of truth; courses and industry libraries are curated views over
them.
```

Two mechanical requirements are easy to miss. The file must be served from the
**root** of the domain — `example.com/llms.txt`, never a subdirectory — and it
should be served as plain text or markdown, not rendered as an HTML page. And
because it is hand-curated by definition, it goes stale: if you generate it from
your content layer, as this site does, it stays correct for free. If you write it
by hand, put a reminder in your calendar or accept that it will drift.

<Callout variant="warning" title="Do not put anything in llms.txt you would not publish">
  <p>
    llms.txt is a public file at a predictable URL. It offers no access control,
    no obfuscation, and no privacy. Listing an unlinked page there makes it more
    discoverable, not less — the same mistake people make when they name private
    directories in robots.txt.
  </p>
</Callout>

## What are the common mistakes in an llms.txt file?

The most common mistake is treating llms.txt as a sitemap — dumping every URL you
have into it — which destroys the only property that makes the format worth
anything. Curation is the feature. A file listing forty pages with real
descriptions is more useful than one listing four hundred without them, because
the point is to tell a model what to read *instead of* everything else.

The second mistake is writing descriptions as marketing copy. "The industry's
leading guide to AI search" tells a model nothing; "explains how retrieval
grounds an AI answer in source passages" tells it exactly when this page is the
right one. Write each description as the answer to *what question does this page
settle*, in the same plain, answer-first voice you should be using on the pages
themselves.

Third: linking to pages that are not actually your best work, or that are gated,
redirected, or slow. The file is a recommendation, and a recommendation is only as
good as what it points at. Fourth: forgetting it exists. A curated index that
describes your site as it was eighteen months ago is worse than no index, because
it actively misrepresents you. Generating the file from your content layer at
build time removes this failure mode entirely — the file cannot drift from
content it is derived from.

Finally, do not build llms.txt before the layers beneath it work. If AI crawlers
are blocked, or your pages render only in JavaScript, a curated markdown index
changes nothing about your visibility. Access first, always.

## Who actually honors llms.txt today?

No major AI answer engine has confirmed that it reads llms.txt, and server-log
evidence indicates crawlers essentially ignore it. That is the short answer, and
the long one — with the log data and Google's public comparison of llms.txt to the
long-dead keywords meta tag — belongs to
[does llms.txt actually work?](/learn/does-llms-txt-work). If you are deciding
whether to invest, read that piece before this section persuades you of anything.

What does consume it, today, is narrower and more mundane: people and tools that
fetch the file *deliberately*. A developer pasting a docs site's llms.txt into a
chat, an agent configured to pull a known URL, an internal script building context
for a model — these work because a human pointed at the file, not because an
engine discovered it. That is a real use case. It is just not the same as
organic AI visibility, and conflating the two is how llms.txt acquired its
reputation as an AEO tactic.

## Is llms.txt worth the effort?

It is worth roughly ten minutes and a place in your build pipeline — no more. The
honest cost-benefit is that publishing one is cheap, carries no downside, may help
the occasional deliberate consumer, and positions you if adoption ever arrives;
while the benefit to your AI citations today is, on the available evidence, zero.

The failure mode is not the file. It is the opportunity cost. Every hour spent
perfecting a curated index that nothing fetches is an hour not spent on the things
that demonstrably decide whether you get cited: making sure AI crawlers can
actually reach and render your pages
([check whether AI crawlers can read your site](/learn/check-ai-crawlers-can-read-site)),
writing answer-first passages that survive being lifted out of context, and
earning the independent authority that engines weigh far above anything you
declare about yourself — the [Access pillar](/pillars/access) and everything
above it in [The AEO Canon](/learn/aeo-canon).

So: generate one, ship it, forget it. Then go do the work in
[what is AEO](/learn/what-is-aeo). And if you are auditing your stack for other
plausible-sounding tactics that do not hold up, start with
[AI search optimization myths, debunked](/learn/aeo-myths).
