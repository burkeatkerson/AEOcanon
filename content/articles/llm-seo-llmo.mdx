**LLM SEO (LLMO) is the practice of structuring and writing content so a large
language model can reach it, understand it, and reproduce it accurately in a
generated answer.** The acronym is one of several floating around for the same
goal, but the label matters less than the reason it exists: a language model
does not browse your site the way a person does or index it the way a search
crawler does. It reaches you through a pipeline, and every stage of that pipeline
implies something concrete about how a page should be written.

## What are the two paths an LLM takes to your content?

An LLM reaches your content by one of two paths: it was absorbed during
training, or it is retrieved live at the moment someone asks a question. These
are genuinely different mechanisms with different timescales, and conflating them
is the most common source of confusion in this field.

The training path is why a model can describe your company without searching for
it — and why it can describe you wrongly and confidently, since nothing in a
trained model is a lookup. It is also why the model's
[knowledge cutoff](/glossary/knowledge-cutoff) matters: the corpus is a frozen
snapshot, explained in [what is training data](/learn/what-is-training-data) and
[how knowledge cutoff affects freshness](/learn/how-knowledge-cutoff-affects-freshness).

The retrieval path is [retrieval-augmented generation](/learn/what-is-rag), the
architecture behind essentially every AI answer engine that shows citations. It
is the path you can influence in a normal planning cycle, which is why the rest
of this article follows it stage by stage. If you want the model itself explained
first, start with [what is an LLM](/learn/what-is-an-llm) and
[how LLMs work](/learn/how-llms-work).

<Callout variant="insight" title="Why both paths point at the same work">
  <p>
    Content that is clearly written, well structured, and consistently repeated
    across the web does better on <em>both</em> paths. Retrieval favors passages
    that stand alone and carry evidence; training favors claims that appear often
    enough, and consistently enough, to become the pattern a model settles on.
    There is no version of LLMO where you optimize for one at the expense of the
    other.

## How does the retrieval pipeline actually work?

The retrieval pipeline turns a question into a cited answer in five stages:
the query is embedded, candidate passages are retrieved, those candidates are
reranked, the winners are fed to the model as context, and the model generates an
answer that attributes what it used. Every stage is a filter — content that fails
at stage two never reaches stage four, no matter how well written it is.

Before any of that runs, your page has to be processed into a retrievable form.
That preparation is where most LLMO leverage actually sits, because it decides
what the retrievable units even are.

Read those two diagrams together and the shape of the discipline appears. You do
not optimize a page for an LLM. You optimize the chunks a page will be cut into,
so that each one survives extraction, embeds cleanly, wins a similarity search,
holds up under reranking, and reads as a complete thought when quoted.

## What do tokenization and chunking imply for writing?

Tokenization and chunking imply that the retrievable unit of your content is a
few hundred words, not a page — so every few hundred words should make sense on
their own. Models read [tokens](/glossary/token), not characters or words, and
retrieval systems slice documents into chunks sized to fit those token budgets.

[Tokenization](/learn/what-is-tokenization) is the step that converts text into
the numeric units a model processes. Its practical implication is quiet but real:
clear, conventional prose tokenizes efficiently, while dense abbreviation,
inconsistent naming, and unusual formatting consume more of a limited budget to
convey the same meaning. Spelling your brand and product names consistently
across a site is a small act with outsized effect — it gives the model one stable
pattern to attach to instead of three competing ones.

[Chunking](/learn/what-is-chunking) is where the real leverage is. A system that
splits your article on a fixed token count will cut wherever the count lands,
possibly mid-argument. A system using
[semantic chunking](/glossary/semantic-chunking) splits on meaning — most often
at heading boundaries, because headings are the strongest available signal of
where one idea ends and the next begins. You do not control which method an
engine uses, but you control how much damage the crude one can do.

## How do embeddings and retrieval decide what gets pulled?

Embeddings and retrieval decide what gets pulled by comparing the meaning of a
question to the meaning of your passages, not their wording. Each chunk is
converted into an [embedding](/glossary/embeddings) — a vector that positions the
text in a high-dimensional space where semantically similar things sit close
together. The query is embedded the same way, and
[retrieval](/glossary/retrieval) returns the nearest neighbors.

This is the stage that most changes how you think about keywords. Because
similarity is computed on meaning, a passage can be retrieved for a question that
uses none of its words, and can be skipped despite containing the exact phrase.
[Cosine similarity](/glossary/cosine-similarity) does not reward repetition; it
rewards a passage whose meaning is tightly clustered around one clear idea. The
practical consequence is the opposite of keyword density — a chunk that says one
thing clearly beats a chunk that says four things while mentioning the target
phrase six times.

Most production systems do not rely on embeddings alone. They run
[hybrid search](/glossary/hybrid-search), combining
[dense retrieval](/glossary/dense-retrieval) with a lexical method like
[BM25](/glossary/bm25) so that exact terms — product names, error codes, statute
numbers, model numbers — still match reliably. That is the one place literal
phrasing retains real force: use the exact name of the thing at least once,
somewhere a chunk boundary will not orphan it. The fuller comparison is in
[semantic search vs keyword search](/learn/semantic-search-vs-keyword-search).

## What does reranking reward, and how do you win it?

Reranking rewards passages that are not just topically close but genuinely the
best available answer — judged on relevance, authority, and freshness together.
Retrieval is fast and shallow; it casts a wide net. The
[reranker](/learn/what-is-a-reranker) is slow and careful, examining the query and
each candidate passage jointly rather than comparing precomputed vectors.

That joint examination, usually performed by a
[cross-encoder](/glossary/cross-encoder), is why reranking is the stage where
quality signals bite. The model is effectively asking: does this passage
actually answer this question, and can I trust it enough to repeat it? Passages
that assert without evidence, hedge without committing, or answer a slightly
different question get demoted here even after retrieving well.

This is also the stage the empirical research speaks to most directly. The
Princeton-led GEO study (arXiv 2311.09735) tested optimization tactics against a
benchmark of real queries and found that adding authoritative citations, direct
quotations, and relevant statistics lifted a source's visibility in generated
answers by up to 40%, while keyword stuffing produced no gain. Those three
winning tactics are exactly what a reranker is built to notice: a citation
signals [grounding](/glossary/grounding), a quotation supplies attributable text,
and a statistic supplies a checkable fact.

Authority arrives at this stage from off-site too. Ahrefs' study of 75,000
brands found off-site brand mentions correlated with AI visibility at 0.664,
against 0.218 for backlinks — so being discussed across the web changes which of
several adequate passages gets promoted. That is the argument developed in
[does E-E-A-T matter for AI citation](/learn/does-eeat-matter-for-ai-citation).

<Callout variant="tip" title="A fast reranker self-test">
  <p>
    Take any passage on your site and read it alone, with no page around it. Ask:
    does it state its answer in the first sentence? Does it name the entities it
    refers to? Does it support its central claim with a source, number, or
    quotation that is <em>inside the passage</em>? Would a careful editor repeat
    it verbatim? Four yeses is a passage that survives reranking.
  </p>
</Callout>

## What happens at generation, and how does that change your writing?

At generation, the model writes an answer using the retrieved passages as
context — which means your text stops being something to rank and becomes
something to be reproduced. The retrieved chunks enter the model's
[context window](/glossary/context-window) alongside the question and the system
instructions, and the answer is composed from that material.

Two properties decide whether your passage survives into the final text. The
first is **liftability**: a sentence that is complete, declarative, and free of
context-dependent references can be reused nearly verbatim, while one that
depends on setup has to be rewritten — and rewriting is where attribution gets
lost. The second is **safety**: models are tuned to avoid stating things they
cannot support, so a claim that arrives with a source attached is far likelier to
be repeated than an unsupported assertion of the same fact.

Generation is also where things go wrong in ways you should anticipate. A model
working from thin or ambiguous context will fill gaps —
[hallucination](/glossary/hallucination) is likelier when the retrieved passages
do not clearly answer the question. Ambiguous writing on your side raises the
odds of being paraphrased into something you did not say. Precision is not
pedantry here; it is defensive.

The shape of that funnel — not the specific widths, which are illustrative — is
the argument for working the pipeline in order. Content that fails at ingestion
never gets a chance at reranking, and content that survives reranking but reads
as a fragment gets paraphrased instead of quoted.
