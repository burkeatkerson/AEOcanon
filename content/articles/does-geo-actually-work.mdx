**Yes — but the evidence base is narrower than the industry's confidence
suggests, and saying so is the point of this article.** There is one good
controlled study, one large correlation study, some solid behavioral data, and a
great deal of vendor content dressed as research. That is enough to justify
acting, and not enough to justify certainty.

This piece is an evidence review rather than a pitch. Every figure below appears
elsewhere on this site, and here the job is to say what each study actually
measured, what it cannot support, and what a rational person should conclude when
the honest answer is "probably, and here is how we'd know."

## Does GEO actually work?

GEO works in the sense that the tactics it recommends have measurably improved a
source's visibility in generative answers under test conditions — and it is
unproven in the stronger sense that anyone can promise you a specific outcome on
a specific engine today. Those two statements are both true, and most content on
this topic picks one and hides the other.

What we can say with confidence is narrower than "GEO works": adding inline
evidence to content increased how prominently that content appeared in generated
answers in a controlled benchmark, and brands with more off-site mentions appear
more often in AI answers at scale. Neither finding is trivial. Neither is a
guarantee that a given page will get cited next Tuesday. If you want the
definition and origin of the term before the evidence, start with
[what is GEO?](/learn/what-is-geo).

## What evidence do we actually have?

We have three distinct kinds of evidence, and they are not equally strong. In
descending order of rigor: one controlled experiment on a purpose-built
benchmark, one large-scale observational correlation study, and a body of
behavioral data about how users interact with AI answers. Nothing here is a
randomized trial on the live web, because no one can run one.

The bottom row deserves its own warning. Most "GEO works!" content circulating
online is a vendor describing a client whose citations went up, with no control
group, no accounting for concurrent changes, and no published methodology. That
is not evidence of anything except that citations sometimes go up. Treat it as
hypothesis, never as proof — and note that AI citations are noisy enough on their
own that they fluctuate week to week without anyone doing anything.

## What did the Princeton GEO study actually measure?

The Princeton-led GEO study measured the change in a source's visibility inside a
generated answer after applying specific content edits to that source, across a
benchmark of roughly 10,000 real queries.
[The paper (arXiv 2311.09735)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2311.09735) introduced both
the term GEO and **GEO-Bench**, and it is the only widely cited work in this
field that tested tactics rather than asserting them.

Two things make this study unusually valuable. First, it manipulated the input
and observed the output, which is the difference between an experiment and an
observation. Second, its negative result is as informative as its positive ones:
keyword stuffing reduced visibility by about 10%, which tells us the mechanism is
not term matching. Content that reads as credible and verifiable wins; content
that reads as optimized loses. That finding is the empirical basis for the
[Credibility pillar](/pillars/credibility) and for the practice described in
[using statistics, quotes, and citations](/learn/statistics-quotes-citations-aeo).

<Callout variant="warning" title="What the GEO study cannot tell you">
  <p>
    <strong>It was a benchmark, not the open web.</strong> GEO-Bench isolates
    variables by construction — which is what makes it rigorous and also what
    makes it unlike a live query where thousands of competing sources exist.
  </p>
  <p>
    <strong>The visibility metrics were purpose-built.</strong> The researchers
    defined how prominence and answer-share were scored. Reasonable definitions,
    but not an industry standard, and not the same thing as a citation link a user
    can click.
  </p>
  <p>
    <strong>It ran on 2023-era engines.</strong> Retrieval stacks, rerankers, and
    grounding behavior have changed materially since. The findings may hold; they
    have not been re-verified at the same rigor on current systems.
  </p>
</Callout>

The perishability point is the one practitioners underweight. A finding about
search engines from 2023 is a finding about a specific set of models and
retrieval pipelines, and generative engines iterate faster than search engines
ever did. The mechanism the study identified — engines prefer sources that are
safe to repeat — is likely durable. The specific percentages almost certainly are
not.

## What does the Ahrefs correlation study prove?

Ahrefs' study proves that brand mentions and AI visibility move together at
scale, and it cannot prove that mentions cause AI visibility. That distinction is
not pedantry; it changes what you should do with the finding.
[Ahrefs' analysis of 75,000 brands](https://ahrefs.com/blog/ai-overview-brand-correlation/)
found brand web mentions correlated with AI visibility at **0.664**, versus
**0.218 for backlinks** — a striking gap, and the single most-quoted number in
this field.

<PullQuote cite="The correlation problem, stated plainly">
  Big brands get mentioned a lot. Big brands also get cited a lot. The study
  cannot tell you which one is doing the work.
</PullQuote>

The plausible causal stories are at least three. Mentions could cause citations,
if engines weight corroboration across the web. Brand size could cause both, in
which case mentions are a symptom rather than a lever. Or citations could
generate mentions, as AI-recommended brands get discussed more. The correlation
is consistent with all three, and the study design cannot separate them. Anyone
quoting 0.664 as proof that a mention-building campaign will raise your AI
visibility is going past what the data supports.

That said, the finding is still actionable, for a specific reason: under every
one of those causal stories, being genuinely more mentioned is either helpful or
neutral. It is never harmful. That asymmetry — high potential upside, near-zero
downside — is what makes off-site authority work a rational bet even without
causal proof. The [Authority pillar](/pillars/authority) and the
[off-site authority audit](/authority/off-site-authority-audit) are built on that
logic rather than on a claim of proven causation.

## What about the click data — does it prove anything about GEO?

The click data proves the problem exists; it proves nothing about whether GEO
solves it. This is the most common piece of sleight-of-hand in AEO marketing:
citing traffic loss as evidence that a particular remedy works.
[Pew Research found in July 2025](https://www.pewresearch.org/short-reads/2025/07/22/google-users-are-less-likely-to-click-on-links-when-an-ai-summary-appears-in-the-results/)
that users clicked a traditional link just **8% of the time** when an AI summary
appeared versus **15%** without one, and that **58%** encountered at least one AI
summary in a single month. Those numbers establish urgency. They say nothing
about whether your content edits will get you into the summary.

There is adjacent evidence that being cited is worth something once you get
there. Seer Interactive
[found that pages cited inside an AI Overview earned a 35% higher organic
clickthrough rate](https://www.seerinteractive.com/insights/how-ai-overviews-are-impacting-ctr-5-initial-takeaways),
and Ahrefs reported
[AI-search visitors converting roughly 23x better than organic search
visitors](https://ahrefs.com/blog/ai-search-traffic-conversions-ahrefs/) on their
own site. Both are observational and both come from interested parties, but they
point the same direction: the citation appears to be worth having, even if the
volume behind it is small. Notice that this is still not evidence that GEO
tactics get you cited — only that citation, once earned, has value.

## Is AEO just SEO with a new name?

Partly — and the honest answer is a percentage, not a yes or a no. Roughly
**70–80%** of what AEO asks for is what good SEO already asked for:
crawlability, fast pages, clean semantic HTML, accurate content, and real
authority. Anyone claiming AEO is a wholly new discipline is selling something.

The remaining portion is not a rebrand, and the evidence is what distinguishes
it. Engines retrieve and rerank *passages* rather than ranking pages, which makes
passage-level structure a first-class concern in a way it never was for SEO.
Ahrefs' correlation gap (mentions at 0.664 versus backlinks at 0.218) implies a
different weighting of authority signals than link-driven SEO assumed. And the
success metric changes from position to [citation share](/glossary/citation-share),
which is measured differently and behaves differently. That argument is developed
at length in [is AEO just SEO?](/learn/is-aeo-just-seo) and
[is AEO replacing SEO?](/learn/is-aeo-replacing-seo).

## Where should the uncertainty change your behavior?

The uncertainty should change how you sequence and how you report, not whether
you act. A field with one good experiment and one good correlation study is a
field where you should prefer tactics that are robust to being wrong, and where
you should measure your own results rather than trusting anyone's benchmark —
including this site's.

## What should a rational person conclude?

A rational person should conclude that GEO is probably effective, that the
evidence justifies acting, and that anyone offering certainty is either
uninformed or selling. The tactics with the best support are also the tactics you
would want on your site if AI search vanished tomorrow — cite your sources, use
real numbers, quote credible people, write clearly, be crawlable, be genuinely
worth mentioning. That is an unusually comfortable position to be in under
uncertainty: the bet pays off if the thesis is right, and costs you almost
nothing if it is wrong.

What that does not license is the confident promise. No one can tell you that a
given edit will produce a given citation rate, because no one has run the study
that would support it, and the systems change faster than such a study could be
replicated. The correct posture is the one the original researchers modeled: form
a hypothesis, measure it, publish the negative results alongside the positive
ones, and update.
