**Generative engine optimization (GEO) is the practice of optimizing content so
it is surfaced and cited in the responses of generative AI engines.** It is the
research community's name for what the industry calls
[answer engine optimization](/learn/what-is-aeo) — and it comes with something
rare in search: peer-reviewed evidence of which tactics actually move the needle.

## Where does the term GEO come from?

GEO comes from a November 2023 research paper titled
["GEO: Generative Engine Optimization" (arXiv 2311.09735)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2311.09735),
authored by a team led by Princeton University with collaborators from the Allen
Institute for AI, Georgia Tech, and IIT Delhi. It was the first formal,
peer-reviewed framework for optimizing content toward generative engines rather
than traditional search rankings — and it gave the discipline both a name and a
way to measure it.

<Callout variant="definition" title="Generative engine">
  <p>
    The study defines a <strong>generative engine</strong> as a system that uses
    generative models to synthesize a response to a query while drawing on
    multiple web sources — and that can cite them. ChatGPT, Perplexity, Google AI
    Overviews, and Gemini all qualify. GEO is optimization aimed squarely at that
    response.
  </p>
</Callout>

Crucially, the researchers also built **GEO-Bench**, a benchmark of thousands of
real queries across domains, so optimization tactics could be tested rather than
guessed at. That benchmark is why GEO conclusions carry more weight than typical
search folklore — they were measured against a controlled set of generative
responses.

The timing is what made the paper land. It appeared just as generative search
was moving from novelty to default — Google was rolling out its Search
Generative Experience, ChatGPT had added browsing, and Perplexity was growing
fast. Content owners could see traffic patterns shifting but had no framework for
responding. GEO gave them one: a name for the goal, evidence for what works, and
a way to measure progress. It reframed the question from "how do I rank?" to "how
do I get the model to use my content?" — the question that defines the discipline
today.

## What did the GEO study find works?

The GEO study found that adding **citations, quotations, and statistics** to
content produced the largest gains, lifting a source's visibility in generative
responses by **up to 40%.** Improving the fluency and structure of writing helped
as well. The tactics that did _not_ help are as instructive as the ones that did. The
common thread among the winners is that each one makes content easier to _trust_
and easier to _lift_ — a citation signals grounding, a quotation gives the model
attributable text, a statistic supplies a verifiable fact. These are precisely
the qualities a generative engine needs to repeat your claim safely under its own
answer.

<Callout variant="warning" title="What the GEO study found does NOT work">
  <p>
    Keyword stuffing produced no meaningful visibility gain. The single most
    important takeaway of the research is that the tactics of classic
    keyword-era SEO do not transfer to generative engines — credibility and
    evidence do. This is why GEO and AEO both push toward
    <em> better-sourced writing</em>, not denser keywords.
  </p>
</Callout>

These findings are the empirical backbone of modern AEO. They explain why
[how AI engines choose what to cite](/learn/how-ai-engines-choose-citations)
rewards inline evidence, and they translate directly into the practices in
[The AEO Canon](/learn/aeo-canon).

## How did the researchers measure GEO?

The researchers measured GEO by building GEO-Bench — a benchmark of roughly
10,000 real user queries spanning domains like science, history, business, and
health — and then scoring how visible a given source was within the generative
answers those queries produced. Rather than guessing whether a tactic worked,
they applied each optimization to source content, regenerated the answers, and
measured the change in two purpose-built visibility metrics.

Those metrics matter because "visibility" in a generated answer is subtler than
a ranking. One metric weighted the **position and prominence** of a source's
contribution to the answer; another weighted the **proportion of the answer**
influenced by that source. By measuring both, the study could distinguish a
source that was merely mentioned from one that materially shaped the response.
This rigor is why GEO's headline finding — up to a **40% visibility lift** from
citations, quotations, and statistics — carries weight that typical
before-and-after marketing anecdotes do not.

<Callout variant="insight" title="Why a benchmark changes the conversation">
  <p>
    Most search advice is folklore — tactics that "seem to work." GEO-Bench let
    researchers test tactics against a controlled set of generative answers, so
    GEO began life as a <em>measured</em> discipline. That is rare in search, and
    it is why the study's conclusions anchor modern AEO practice.
  </p>
</Callout>

## How is GEO different from AEO?

GEO and AEO describe the same goal with different emphasis: GEO is engine- and
research-centric, AEO is answer- and practitioner-centric. GEO measures the
"visibility" of a source within generated text — what fraction of the response it
influences and whether it is cited. AEO frames the work around the user's
question and the passage that answers it. In everyday practice the two are
interchangeable, and we map the full relationship in
[AEO vs GEO vs SEO](/learn/aeo-vs-geo-vs-seo). You will also encounter adjacent
labels — LLMO, AIO, generative search optimization — but they describe the same
work. Chasing the "correct" term is a distraction; the discipline is defined by
its goal, not its acronym.

<PullQuote cite="GEO in one line">
  GEO is the science; AEO is the practice. Both want your passage inside the
  answer.
</PullQuote>

The reason both terms ultimately point at the same techniques is the shared
machinery underneath every generative engine:
[retrieval-augmented generation](/learn/what-is-rag), the architecture introduced
in
[Lewis et al.'s 2020 paper (arXiv 2005.11401)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2005.11401).
Whether you call it GEO or AEO, you are optimizing for a system that retrieves
passages, reranks them on relevance and authority, and quotes the most credible,
well-evidenced ones.

## Which engines and content does GEO apply to?

GEO applies to every generative engine that synthesizes answers from web sources
— Google AI Overviews and AI Mode, ChatGPT, Perplexity, Gemini, and Microsoft
Copilot — and to any content those engines might draw on to answer a question.
Because all of them share the same retrieval-and-generation machinery, the GEO
tactics that lift visibility in one tend to lift it across the board, which is
what makes GEO a general discipline rather than a per-platform trick.

The content that benefits most is informational: explainers, comparisons,
definitions, how-tos, and data-rich resources — the material people ask
questions about. Transactional and navigational pages benefit less directly,
because users rarely ask an engine to "synthesize an answer" about a checkout
page. The practical implication is to point GEO effort at the questions your
audience asks while researching, where a synthesized answer is most likely to
appear and a citation is most valuable.

## How do you apply GEO?

You apply GEO by turning its tested findings into a writing standard: lead with a
clear answer, support every important claim with a citation, quotation, or
statistic, and write with authority. Then make sure the page is technically
reachable and build the off-site reputation that signals trust —
[Ahrefs' study of 75,000 brands](https://ahrefs.com/blog/ai-overview-brand-correlation/)
found those brand mentions are the single strongest correlate of AI visibility.
That is GEO, AEO, and good content engineering all at once — which is exactly why
this site treats them as one discipline.

A simple way to operationalize GEO is to audit your most important pages against
the study's findings, one question at a time. Does each passage answer its
question in the first sentence? Is every meaningful claim backed by a citation,
quotation, or statistic? Is the source current, and is the author credible? Where
the answer is no, you have found a concrete, evidence-based improvement — not a
guess. Fixing those gaps is GEO in practice, and because the tactics generalize
across engines, the same edits improve your visibility in ChatGPT, Perplexity,
and Google AI Overviews at once.
