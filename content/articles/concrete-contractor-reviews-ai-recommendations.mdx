## Why do reviews carry so much weight for concrete contractors?

Because they're the off-site proof an engine uses to judge trust — and a pour is a durable,
expensive, one-shot commitment. An assistant deciding whom to recommend [synthesizes
sentiment](/learn/do-reviews-affect-ai-recommendations) from your Google and third-party reviews to
gauge whether your work holds up, your crew communicates, and you deliver on time and budget. For a
trade where a cracked driveway or a failed patio is a costly redo, that corroborated reputation is
decisive — it's the [Credibility pillar](/pillars/credibility) made visible, and the [off-site
mentions](/learn/mentions-vs-backlinks) that correlate with AI visibility more than backlinks do.

## How many reviews do I need?

Fewer than you think, fresher than you have. There's no magic count — consistency and recency outweigh
a raw total. A steady stream of genuine recent reviews signals an active, trusted contractor far
better than a big pile from three years ago. The goal is an ongoing flow: build a simple habit of
asking at job completion, so new reviews keep arriving and the engine keeps seeing a contractor that's
[currently trusted](/learn/local-reviews-and-ai-recommendations), not formerly busy.

## Do the words in a review matter?

Yes — specific reviews do more work than generic praise.

Reviews that name what you did — poured a driveway on budget, finished a stamped patio with a clean
edge, built a retaining wall that's held up through the freeze-thaw — give engines specific detail
tying you to those exact jobs and to durable, reliable work. They reinforce the [questions homeowners
ask](/learn/concrete-masonry-questions-customers-ask-ai), so a handful of detailed reviews can
outperform a wall of generic ones. Ask clients what to mention, and the reviews start doing your AEO
for you.
