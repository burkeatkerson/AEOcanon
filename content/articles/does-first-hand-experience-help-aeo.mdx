## Why does experience-based content stand out?

Because it can't be replicated. Anyone can paraphrase common knowledge — and
increasingly, so can a language model — but only you can describe what actually
happened when you did the thing. That specificity is both an
[originality](/pillars/originality) signal and a [credibility](/pillars/credibility)
one: it marks genuine expertise and gives the engine a unique, trustworthy source
rather than [another interchangeable summary](/learn/will-ai-cite-generic-content).

## How do I show it in content?

Through concrete specifics, not abstractions. Include the actual numbers, examples,
mistakes, and outcomes from real experience — describe what you did, what happened,
and what you learned. The detail that only a practitioner would know is what
[demonstrates expertise](/learn/how-to-show-expertise-to-ai) and marks the content as
genuine rather than generic. Vague "best practices" anyone could list don't carry the
signal; the specific story does.

## Is experience more valuable than research?

They're complementary, and strongest together. [Original
research](/learn/does-original-research-get-cited-more) gives you unique data;
first-hand experience gives you unique insight and detail. Both make content
[irreplaceable](/learn/how-to-make-content-ai-cant-ignore), and the most citable pages
often combine them — lived experience interpreting original data is something no
competitor and no model can match, which is about as unignorable as content gets.
