## Why are case studies so citable?

Because they're original and evidenced at once. A real case study is something only
you experienced, so it carries the distinctiveness of [first-hand
experience](/learn/does-first-hand-experience-help-aeo) *and* the concrete, [unique
data](/learn/does-original-research-get-cited-more) of a measurable outcome — the
exact combination engines reward. That makes it a natural [Originality
pillar](/pillars/originality) asset: distinctive, credible, and impossible for a
competitor to replicate.

## What separates a citable case study from a useless one?

Specifics. The citable version has actual numbers, the real approach, the timeline,
and a verifiable result; the useless version is a vague "we helped a client succeed"
with nothing concrete. The more specific and verifiable the outcome, the more it
functions as data an engine can [lift and
attribute](/learn/statistics-quotes-citations-aeo) — and the more it reads as genuine
rather than marketing. Detail is the whole difference.

## How should I structure one?

Front-load the result, then make each part liftable. Lead with the headline outcome as
a [self-contained statistic](/learn/answer-first-sentence), then break the situation,
approach, and result into clear passages with specific numbers — each
[standing on its own](/learn/should-content-sections-stand-alone). Put it in crawlable
HTML and attribute the results to yourself. Don't build suspense toward the outcome;
state it first, because that's the part the engine will quote.
