**AI recognizes entities by linking the names it reads to unique items in a
knowledge graph, using context and embeddings to disambiguate which thing is meant,
then drawing on that entity's attributes and corroboration to judge trust.**
Recognition, disambiguation, and trust are three distinct steps — and you can
influence each one.

## How does an engine link a name to an entity?

An engine links a name to an entity through entity linking: it spots a mention in
the text and matches it against a [knowledge graph](/glossary/knowledge-graph) of known things, each with a
stable identifier, attributes, and relationships. This is the machinery behind
Google's "things, not strings" [Knowledge Graph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Google_Knowledge_Graph), and AI answer engines reason the
same way. The link is what turns the string "Acme Analytics" into *the* company with
a founding date, a category, named people, and a trust profile — everything the
engine needs to decide whether to rely on you.

## How does it disambiguate same-named things?

It handles [disambiguation](/glossary/disambiguation) using context and learned representations. When a name could mean
several things, the engine weighs the surrounding words and the candidate entities'
known relationships to choose the right one — and [embeddings](/learn/what-are-embeddings),
which place words and entities in a numeric space where related things cluster, let
it do this even without an exact match. The practical consequence: if your entity is
thinly or inconsistently described, you're easy to confuse with a similarly-named
thing, and the engine may link the mention to the wrong entity — or to none. Clear,
consistent, distinctive signals are how you win the disambiguation. This is the same
retrieval-and-reasoning stack described in [how LLMs work](/learn/how-llms-work) and
[how AI engines choose citations](/learn/how-ai-engines-choose-citations).

## How does recognition become trust?

Once an engine has linked you to a known entity, it judges trust from what's
attached to that entity — its attributes, its [corroboration](/glossary/corroboration) across reputable
sources, and its track record. An entity that trusted sources describe consistently
is a safe one to surface; an unrecognized or contested entity is not. This is why
entity recognition is the precondition for the [authority](/pillars/authority) and
[credibility](/pillars/credibility) pillars: trust has to attach to *something the
engine can identify*. You can't earn authority as a string.
