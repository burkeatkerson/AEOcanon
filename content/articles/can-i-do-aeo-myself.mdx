## What can I do on my own?

The core, highest-impact work. Writing [answer-first
passages](/learn/answer-first-sentence), using [question-shaped
headings](/learn/question-shaped-headings), keeping content accurate and fresh, and
[confirming AI crawlers can read your pages](/learn/check-ai-crawlers-can-read-site) are
all within reach of a non-specialist — and they happen to be the
[cheapest, highest-impact moves](/learn/cheapest-high-impact-aeo-moves). Doing them
yourself captures most of the available value, which is why a [30-day
plan](/learn/30-day-aeo-plan) is realistic for a solo owner.

## When should I get help?

For specialized or large-scale work. Complex technical rendering fixes, [migrating a
JavaScript-heavy site](/learn/make-spa-citable), or running [authority and PR
campaigns at scale](/learn/build-ai-authority-from-scratch) are where outside expertise
earns its keep — especially if you lack the time or specific skills. If you'd rather
not learn those, that's the natural place to bring in [an agency or a
specialist](/learn/do-i-need-an-agency-for-aeo). The everyday content work usually
isn't.

## Is DIY enough to actually get cited?

Often, yes — especially on less competitive questions. Doing the fundamentals well —
crawlable, [answer-first](/learn/answer-first-sentence), evidenced, [original
content](/learn/how-to-make-content-ai-cant-ignore) — earns citations without any
agency. Highly competitive topics may need more sustained
[authority](/pillars/authority) work over time, but DIY gets most sites meaningfully
further than owners expect, and you can always add help where you hit a real ceiling.
