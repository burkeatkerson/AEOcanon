**HTTPS is a baseline expectation, not a differentiator — having it won't boost your
AI citations, but lacking it is a credibility and security red flag that can undercut
trust.** Treat HTTPS as table stakes you must have, then earn trust through accuracy,
sourcing, and corroboration.

## Does HTTPS earn me citations?

No — it just keeps you from losing trust. HTTPS is so universally expected that having
it is invisible; it's a hygiene factor, not a [credibility](/pillars/credibility)
differentiator. What it does is remove a *negative* — an insecure site reads as
careless and risky — so switching to HTTPS earns you the absence of distrust, not a
citation bump. The real levers lie elsewhere.

## Is HTTP-only actually a problem?

Yes, and an avoidable one. An HTTP-only site triggers browser security warnings and
undercuts trust in everything on it, which works against both users and engines. It's
a foundational, low-effort fix, so there's no reason to leave it unaddressed if you
want your content taken seriously — it sits alongside [crawlability and
speed](/pillars/access) as basic site hygiene.

## What basic trust signals do I need?

The hygiene set, then the substance. HTTPS, a clear identity (real
[organization and author](/learn/do-author-bios-help-aeo) information), accurate and
current content, and [credible sourcing](/learn/does-citing-sources-help-get-cited)
are the factors that create distrust when missing but don't differentiate you when
present. Get them right as a baseline, then compete on the things that actually earn
citations — [demonstrated expertise](/learn/how-to-show-expertise-to-ai) and
[authority](/learn/build-ai-authority-from-scratch).
