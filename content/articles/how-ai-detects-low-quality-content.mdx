**AI engines flag low-quality content through signals of thinness, genericness,
inaccuracy, and lack of corroboration — content that says nothing specific, can't be
verified, or contradicts trusted sources is risky to cite, so engines pass it over.**
The defense is depth, accuracy, originality, and credible sourcing.

## What signals low quality to an engine?

Vagueness and the absence of substance. Generic claims anyone could make, no
[evidence or sourcing](/learn/does-citing-sources-help-get-cited), no original insight,
and no corroboration from trusted sources all mark content as low-value — as does
[keyword stuffing](/learn/keyword-stuffing-ai) and contradicting well-established
facts. Each tells the engine the page is unsafe or pointless to quote, the inverse of
the [credibility](/pillars/credibility) signals it's looking for.

## Is thin content penalized or ignored?

Mostly ignored — which lands the same way. An engine doesn't punish thin content so
much as find nothing in it worth lifting and no reason to trust it over richer
sources, so it simply isn't cited. The effect is invisibility, identical in practice
to a penalty, but it stems from the content offering nothing citable rather than from
a punishment. Either way, the cure is to give the engine something substantial.

## How do I read as high-quality?

Be specific, evidenced, original, and accurate. [Answer the real question
directly](/learn/answer-first-sentence), back claims with [credible
sources](/learn/does-citing-sources-help-get-cited), add [insight only you can
offer](/learn/how-to-make-content-ai-cant-ignore), and keep facts current and
consistent with trusted references. Quality, to an engine, is just substance it can
lift and trust — so produce exactly that, and the low-quality flags never fire.
