**My prediction: by 2028, AI answers increasingly pay or license the sources they
draw from — and the citations flow to publishers that are trusted, licensable,
and well-structured.** Cloudflare's move from Pay Per Crawl toward "pay per
answer," plus Perplexity sharing revenue with publishers, are the early proof
that the free-scraping era is closing. This is a forward-looking bet, but the
2026 signals are unusually concrete.

<Callout variant="key" title="The prediction">
  <p>
    The open web gets funded differently: less by ads and clicks, more by AI
    engines paying or licensing the sources they cite. That tilts the advantage
    toward content that's owned, trusted, and structured for attribution — and
    away from content that's cheap, unowned, or indistinguishable.
  </p>
</Callout>

## What's driving this?

A real shift in the economics of being crawled. Cloudflare launched Pay Per
Crawl to let sites charge AI companies for access, and is steering toward a
world where answers — not just crawls — carry a cost. Perplexity built a
publisher revenue-share program, reportedly a pool around $42.5 million sending
roughly 80% to partners like CNN, Condé Nast, and The Washington Post. Layer on
licensing deals, lawsuits over training data, and rising regulatory pressure on
attribution, and "answers should pay their sources" starts looking inevitable.

## What could this look like by 2028?

Engines preferentially citing sources they've licensed or can pay cleanly, the
way streaming services favor catalogs they've secured rights to. That makes
[citation](/glossary/citation) partly a commercial relationship, not just a
quality signal. Trusted, well-structured publishers get paid and cited; anonymous
or duplicative content gets neither. For businesses, the value of owning
authoritative content on your own domain goes up, because that's what's
licensable and attributable.
