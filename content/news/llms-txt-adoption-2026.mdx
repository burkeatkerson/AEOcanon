**After roughly eighteen months of industry conversation, an SE Ranking study of
300,000 domains found a [llms.txt](/glossary/llms-txt) adoption rate of 10.13% —
about one in ten sites. But AI search crawlers are almost never fetching it.**
[GPTBot](/glossary/gptbot), ClaudeBot, PerplexityBot, OAI-SearchBot, and
Google-Extended overwhelmingly skip /llms.txt and crawl your HTML directly. The
file is real; its role in AI search is not.

Adoption sounds respectable until you check who is reading the file. SE Ranking's
crawl data shows the major AI search agents fetch HTML pages and skip /llms.txt
almost entirely — so the standard is spreading faster than it is being consumed
by the engines it was pitched to help.

## So where does llms.txt actually work?

In the agentic layer. llms.txt has become the first widely-adopted
business-to-agent ("B2A") standard that IDE and coding agents — Cursor, Windsurf,
Claude Code, GitHub Copilot, Cline, and Aider — routinely fetch to orient
themselves in a codebase or product doc. That is real utility, just not the AI
search citation utility it is often sold as. This matches the stance in
[does llms.txt work](/learn/does-llms-txt-work).

## What should you do about it?

Keep llms.txt if you want the developer-agent benefit — it is cheap to maintain
via your [access](/pillars/access) setup — but do not expect it to raise AI
visibility. Put the effort where citations are actually decided:
[how AI engines choose citations](/learn/how-ai-engines-choose-citations).
